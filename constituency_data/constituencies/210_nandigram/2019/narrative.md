## §1 Who lives here

### Geography and administrative composition

Nandigram Assembly Constituency (AC 210) is located in the **Purba Medinipur district** (East Midnapore) of West Bengal. It is part of the Tamluk Lok Sabha constituency. The constituency is composed of **Nandigram I** and **Nandigram II** community development (CD) blocks, covering a combined area of roughly 340 sq km of the Haldi River delta, deltaic floodplain, and low coastal scrubland.

Nandigram I CD Block sits across the Haldi River from the industrial port city of Haldia, bounded by Haldia CD Block to the north. The blocks contain 10 gram panchayats, roughly 98 inhabited villages, and close to 99 mouzas in Nandigram I alone. The district headquarters Tamluk is about 40 km away.

Total estimated population (2011 Census, both CD blocks): **approximately 331,054**. The constituency is overwhelmingly rural — about 96.65% rural, 3.35% urban. Literacy rate in Nandigram I is approximately 84.89%.

### Religious and caste composition

The constituency is the most Muslim-dense seat in Purba Medinipur district:

- **Nandigram I CD Block (2011 Census):** Hindus 65.82% (136,789), Muslims 34.04% (70,756). Scheduled Castes: 38,619 (18.58%). Scheduled Tribes: negligible (0.07%).
- **Nandigram II CD Block:** Muslims are a smaller minority — approximately 12.13% (around 1,940 in a block population of ~123,219). The Hindu and SC proportions are correspondingly higher.

Aggregating across the full constituency, Muslims form roughly **28–34% of total voters**, with the bulk concentrated in Nandigram I block. The constituency-level Muslim share is commonly cited as approximately **30–34%** — a proportion large enough to be decisive in close elections but not numerically dominant. This demographic arithmetic shapes every major political calculation in the seat.

### Communities and sub-identities

**Bengali Hindu majority** — cultivators, artisans, fishing communities, and households linked to the Haldia industrial corridor. Dominant castes include Mahishya (peasant-landowner caste historically strong in coastal Midnapore), alongside Scheduled Caste groups primarily from fishing and agricultural labourer backgrounds. Namasudra, Bagdi, and Kaibarta fishermen constitute a meaningful SC share across the two blocks.

**Muslim population** — largely Bengali-speaking cultivators and smallholders long-settled along the Haldi delta. The term **bhumi putra** (sons of the soil) was prominently used in the 2007 land-agitation discourse to describe all agrarian communities — Hindu and Muslim — who feared displacement by the SEZ. Within the Muslim community, the **Gazi** community (a hereditary clan associated with Islamic saints of the Sundarbans-adjacent Bengal delta) has cultural resonance in the coastal belt; local mosques and dargahs serve as community anchors. Sheikh is the most common Muslim surname in the constituency; other prominent lineages include Khan and Biswas.

**Scheduled Caste fishermen** — Kaibarta and allied communities are numerically significant in both CD blocks. Many are engaged in coastal or riverine fishing, or have shifted to **prawn/shrimp aquaculture** as a primary livelihood in the Haldi and nearby estuaries. SC fishermen communities have historically oscillated between Left Front patronage (the Left built its rural base partly through SC land-reform beneficiaries) and TMC welfare delivery.

**Haldia corridor workers** — a stratum of households in the northern fringe of Nandigram I block that commutes into Haldia's industrial estate: daily-wage contract workers at the Indian Oil Corporation's Haldia Refinery (8 million tonnes per year capacity, commissioned 1975), Haldia Petrochemicals Ltd (HPL, naphtha cracker complex, production from 2000), fertiliser plants, and the Haldia Dock Complex (a major sub-port of Syama Prasad Mookerjee Port, Kolkata). These households occupy a hybrid rural-industrial economic position and were historically unionised through CITU (Centre of Indian Trade Unions, the CPI(M)-aligned union) during the Left Front era.

## §2 The 2007 wound that never healed

### Background: the chemical hub proposal

In late 2006, the Left Front government of West Bengal under Chief Minister Buddhadeb Bhattacharjee identified Nandigram for a **Special Economic Zone (SEZ)** to host a chemical hub by the **Salim Group of Indonesia**. The plan required compulsory acquisition of approximately **10,000 acres** of agricultural land — paddy fields and prawn ponds — farmed by households in Nandigram I and II blocks.

For the peasant communities of Nandigram, this was not an abstract policy debate. The land was their livelihood and ancestral inheritance. The Left Front had originally come to power in 1977 partly on a platform of protecting sharecroppers (Operation Barga) and preventing forcible land displacement. The proposal to hand over 10,000 acres to an Indonesian conglomerate felt like a fundamental betrayal of that founding compact.

### Formation of the BUPC and blockade

Around **January 6, 2007**, farmers and residents — Hindu and Muslim together — formed the **Bhumi Uchhed Pratirodh Committee** (BUPC: Committee Against Land Eviction). The BUPC included 14 founding members, among them **Meghnad Pal** (Hindu), **Sheikh Sufiyan** (Muslim), **Swadesh Das**, **Khokon Shith**, and **Abu Taher** — all residents of Nandigram.

The BUPC physically **blockaded roads leading into the region** from January through March 2007, preventing CPI(M) party workers and police from entering. An estimated 5,000 villagers participated in the blockade at any given time. The resistance crossed religious lines: Hindu Mahishya farmers stood alongside Muslim smallholders in blocking state authority.

Trinamool Congress, then in opposition, supported the BUPC. **Mamata Banerjee** visited Nandigram and publicly championed the farmers, making the Nandigram agitation a central plank of her campaign against the Left Front.

### March 14, 2007: the firing

On **14 March 2007**, the state government directed a police operation to break BUPC's control of Nandigram. Over **3,000 police personnel** were deployed. News of the operation leaked; around **5,000 villagers** assembled at the entrances to block the advance.

Police opened fire. Official figures acknowledged **14 villagers killed** — including two women — with around **70–75 injured**. The Trinamool Congress claimed the death toll was higher; over 100 persons were listed as "missing" in the immediate aftermath. Human rights tribunals documented names and ages of those killed, drawn from both Hindu and Muslim families. Among those documented: **Imadul Khan** (Muslim, 17 years old), son of Abdul Daiyun Khan, a five-acre paddy farmer; members of the **Mondal, Das**, and **Mandal** Hindu families.

Chief Minister Bhattacharjee's reaction was politically catastrophic: he reportedly stated that the opposition had been "paid back in the same coin," appearing to endorse state violence against unarmed peasants. The statement became an enduring symbol of the Left Front's moral collapse.

### The slogan that defined an era: *Ma Mati Manush*

Mamata Banerjee adopted the phrase **"Ma Mati Manush"** (Mother, Motherland, People) as the unifying slogan of her political platform, crystallising the emotional register of the Nandigram agitation into a broader electoral message. She conducted a prolonged **dharna** (sit-in) at Nandigram, refusing to leave until the government backed down.

The government eventually **shelved the SEZ proposal** in early March 2007, before the firing — the firing occurred as the state tried to reassert administrative control of the blockaded area — and the chemical hub project was permanently abandoned. The site was later considered for relocation to Nayachar island.

### Political consequences and folk memory

The **2008 Panchayat elections** delivered a first verdict: the Trinamool Congress won **35 of 53 seats** in the East Midnapore Zilla Parishad, ending CPI(M)'s iron grip on the district council. At the gram panchayat and panchayat samiti levels, the Left Front was "virtually wiped out" in East Midnapore.

In the **2011 state assembly election**, Nandigram returned a Trinamool candidate — **Firoja Bibi** of TMC defeated CPI's Paramananda Bharati with 103,300 votes to 59,660 (margin: 43,640). The Left Front's 34-year rule ended statewide.

By **end-2019**, the events of March 14, 2007 occupy a specific place in Nandigram's collective psyche:

- The date is commemorated locally as **Nandigram Divas** (Nandigram Day); TMC organises commemorative events; martyrs' families are periodically feted.
- **The CPM is associated in folk memory with state violence against farmers.** Among older voters (45+), particularly in Nandigram I block, the memory of the firing produces a visceral anti-Left sentiment that transcends normal party loyalty calculations.
- **The bhumi putra consciousness** — the sense that Nandigram's residents successfully defended their land against an extractive state — is an active identity frame. Political candidates who invoke land rights and sovereignty of small farmers tap into this identity effectively.
- Among the **Muslim families of martyrs** (roughly half the documented dead came from Muslim households), the Nandigram agitation is remembered as a moment of Hindu-Muslim solidarity in defence of land. The TMC has leveraged this memory to cement Muslim loyalty.
- Among **Hindu families of martyrs** whose subsequent expectations of justice and development went unmet, some disillusionment with TMC governance had begun to surface by 2018–19, though the BJP had not yet consolidated this into open defection.

## §3 The 2019 political context

### Tamluk Lok Sabha 2019: TMC holds but BJP surges

The **2019 Lok Sabha election** was fought for Tamluk constituency (which includes Nandigram AC) in the atmosphere of national polarisation over the BJP's Hindutva platform, the Balakot airstrikes (February 2019), and the emerging salience of **CAA/NRC anxiety** among Muslim voters.

**Official result, Tamluk LS 2019:**
- **Dibyendu Adhikari (TMC):** 724,433 votes — **50.08% vote share** — WINNER
- **Sidharthashankar Naskar (BJP):** 534,268 votes — **36.92% vote share**
- **CPI(M):** approximately 9.4% vote share
- Total valid votes: 1,446,499
- TMC winning margin: **190,165 votes**

TMC won, but the BJP's performance — securing nearly 37% in a constituency that had been a Left-then-TMC stronghold — was a significant warning signal. Nationally, the BJP swept 18 of 42 West Bengal seats with approximately 40.7% of the statewide vote share, up from around 11% in 2016. The Left Front's statewide share collapsed to roughly 7.5%, a trend visible in Tamluk as well.

**Dibyendu Adhikari** is Suvendu Adhikari's younger brother — his election as Tamluk MP reflected the continued hold of the Adhikari family across the seat's assembly segments including Nandigram.

### Suvendu Adhikari's standing as of end-2019

**Suvendu Adhikari** is, in December 2019, the MLA for Nandigram (elected 2016 with 134,623 votes; nearest rival CPI's Abdul Kabir Sekh got 53,393 votes — a crushing margin). He simultaneously serves as **West Bengal's Minister of Transport** in Mamata Banerjee's cabinet.

His profile in the constituency as of end-2019:
- Regarded as the **undisputed dada** (strongman-patron) of East Midnapore's 16 assembly segments
- His father **Sisir Adhikari** is MP from Kanthi constituency (re-elected 2019 on TMC ticket)
- His brother **Dibyendu Adhikari** has just won Tamluk LS (2019) for TMC
- **The Adhikari family is the organisational backbone of TMC in the entire Purba Medinipur district**
- At booth level, his network of karyakartas (party workers) is disciplined, loyal, and deeply embedded in gram panchayats and cooperative societies
- He led the 2007 Nandigram agitation as a young TMC leader, cementing his "son of the soil" credentials
- He is **loyal to Mamata Banerjee** as of end-2019; any tension with the party leadership has not yet broken into the public domain

### The 2016 assembly election in Nandigram

2016 result (Nandigram AC):
- **Suvendu Adhikari (AITC/TMC):** 134,623 votes
- **Abdul Kabir Sekh (CPI):** 53,393 votes
- Total valid votes: 201,552; total electorate: 231,866
- TMC won by a margin of ~81,230 votes — a landslide

The BJP barely registered in Nandigram in 2016 (the party's statewide vote share in 2016 was approximately 5.33%). By 2019, the national-level BJP surge under Modi had narrowed this gap considerably across the Tamluk LS constituency, though Nandigram's Muslim concentration (30%+ in Nandigram I block) acted as a moderating factor.

### CAA/NRC anxiety in the Muslim community

The **Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA)** was passed by Parliament on December 11, 2019 — exactly three weeks before this document's temporal freeze. As of late December 2019, Nandigram's Muslim community is deeply anxious. Key sources of concern:

- The CAA grants expedited citizenship to Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, and Christian refugees from Pakistan, Afghanistan, and Bangladesh — but **explicitly excludes Muslims**, framing the community as not persecuted.
- Mamata Banerjee has publicly and forcefully declared she will **not implement** the CAA or any National Register of Citizens (NRC) exercise in West Bengal, and has organised large anti-CAA rallies.
- Among Muslims in Nandigram I block (34% of population), the combination of CAA passage and earlier NRC exercise in Assam (which left 1.9 million people stateless, disproportionately poor Bengalis) has stoked genuine fear of documentation harassment, loss of voting rights, and potential detention.
- Mamata's anti-CAA stand has **reinvigorated Muslim loyalty to TMC** at precisely the moment the BJP was making inroads through Hindu consolidation.
- Suvendu Adhikari, as a TMC minister, publicly supports Mamata's anti-CAA position as of end-2019.

### BJP's 2019 inroads — early signals

BJP's vote surge in Tamluk (from near-zero to 36.92%) in 2019 was driven by several overlapping factors:
- **Hindu consolidation** in response to perceived Muslim appeasement by TMC
- **Anti-incumbency and cut-money complaints** — public anger over TMC karyakartas demanding commissions from PMAY (Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana) housing beneficiaries, MGNREGA wages, and other central scheme disbursals
- **BJP's national narrative** under Modi: Balakot nationalism, anti-corruption, Ram Mandir aspiration
- **CPI(M) voter migration to BJP** — former Left sympathisers, disillusioned with TMC violence and corruption, treating BJP as the available anti-TMC vehicle
- The BJP's 2019 surge was notable even in Nandigram's Hindu-dominated pockets and among some SC communities, though the Muslim bloc held firm for TMC

## §4 Economy and Haldia's shadow

### The Haldia industrial corridor

**Haldia** sits immediately across the Haldi River from Nandigram I block, and its industrial economy casts a long shadow over the constituency's labour market, political culture, and aspirations.

Key Haldia industries:
- **Indian Oil Corporation Haldia Refinery** — capacity 8 million tonnes per year; commissioned 1975; one of the oldest refineries in eastern India; employs several hundred direct workers plus thousands of contract/casual workers
- **Haldia Petrochemicals Ltd (HPL)** — integrated naphtha cracker complex, production from 2000; the first large-scale petrochemical complex in eastern India
- **Haldia Dock Complex (HDC)** — component of Syama Prasad Mookerjee Port, Kolkata; handled record cargo volumes; generates employment in stevedoring, logistics, transport, and ancillary services
- Fertiliser plants and ancillary chemical industries round out the complex

Employment reality: Direct industrial employment from Haldia's heavy industries is significant in absolute numbers but modest relative to Purba Medinipur's large rural population. One estimate from the early 2000s put direct plus downstream employment from Haldia industrial expansion at approximately 13,000–14,000 jobs — a drop in the bucket against 80,000 new labour-market entrants per year in the old undivided Midnapore district. Contract and casual labour is far more common than permanent employment.

**Until 2011**, the dominant labour union at Haldia was **CITU** (Centre of Indian Trade Unions), the CPI(M)-aligned federation. Union politics were inseparable from party politics. After 2011, TMC's INTTUC (Indian National Trinamool Trade Union Congress) gradually displaced CITU's dominance in many Haldia plants. As of 2019, INTTUC is the predominant union, but a cohort of older workers retains Left loyalties.

A significant fraction of Nandigram I block residents — especially from villages close to the Haldi riverbank — work as **daily-wage or contract labourers** at Haldia plants, commuting by ferry or road. These workers exist in a precarious zone: not permanent employees with union benefits, but not purely agricultural either. Their political preferences blend working-class grievances with agrarian concerns.

### Agriculture: paddy and the prawn revolution

**Paddy (rice)** remains the dominant crop in both CD blocks. Nandigram I produced 44,514 tonnes of Aman (kharif) paddy from 23,845 hectares in 2013–14. The coastal deltaic soil is fertile for rice, though high salinity in lower-lying areas constrains to a single annual crop cycle.

**Prawn and shrimp aquaculture** has transformed the local economy over the past two decades:
- An estimated **10,000 people** in the Nandigram block are directly and indirectly engaged in shrimp farming
- Farmers have converted paddy fields to shrimp ponds (bheries) across large parts of Nandigram I's coastal areas
- The main species cultivated include **tiger prawn (Penaeus monodon)** and, increasingly, the cheaper and faster-growing **vannamei**
- East Midnapore became one of the leading prawn-exporting districts in India, with Japan as a key export market for monodon
- **Market volatility is a structural vulnerability**: Japan imposed quality rejections on East Midnapore monodon shipments due to antibiotic and antioxidant residue concerns, causing price collapses that pushed some farmers into debt or suicide
- The prawn economy creates a class of medium-scale entrepreneurs (bhery owners), contract labourers (pond workers), and fish traders — all sensitive to export market access and infrastructure (cold chains, roads to Haldia port)

**Fishing** — both marine and riverine — is the primary occupation of **SC Kaibarta and other fishing communities** in the coastal fringes. Trawler fishing in the Bay of Bengal involves seasonal migration and significant weather risk. The fishing communities are among the most economically marginal in the constituency.

**Small-scale trade and services** in Nandigram town (the constituency's urban node, 3.35% of population) include hardware, agricultural inputs, fish traders, and transport. The town is the administrative hub but is small; it does not generate the kind of service-sector employment that would significantly alter the constituency's agrarian political character.

### Rural-urban and spatial split

The constituency has two distinct political geographies:
- **Nandigram I block (northern, Haldi-riverine, 34% Muslim, higher population density)** — the historically charged zone; site of the 2007 killings; Muslim vote concentration; prawn aquaculture belt; Haldia worker-commuter fringe
- **Nandigram II block (southern, more interior, 12% Muslim, lower population density)** — more uniformly Hindu; higher SC share relative to Muslims; more purely agrarian; BJP's 2019 gains were likely stronger here proportionately

## §5 The TMC patronage network

### The Adhikari machine

The backbone of TMC's organisation in Nandigram is the **Adhikari family network**, built over two decades:
- **Suvendu Adhikari** personally oversees booth-level organisation across Nandigram and beyond
- Booth committees are linked upward through block and district TMC structures that ultimately report to Suvendu
- The network mobilises voters, distributes government scheme benefits, resolves local disputes, and enforces political loyalty through a combination of genuine patronage and implicit coercion

**Gram panchayat capture post-2011:** After the TMC swept panchayat elections in East Midnapore in 2008 and consolidated further in 2013, the ruling party's panchayat pradhans control local development funds, MGNREGA job cards, PMAY housing allocations, and Kanyashree disbursals. In practice, access to these welfare entitlements flows through TMC booth workers and panchayat members. This creates a tangible incentive for voters to maintain visible TMC loyalty.

### Key welfare schemes and their local politics (2014–2019)

- **Kanyashree** — cash transfer to girls aged 13–18 who remain in school and unmarried; launched 2013; won UN Public Service Award 2017; widely popular in rural Bengali households
- **Rupashree** — Rs 25,000 one-time grant to economically weak families at the time of adult daughters' weddings; launched 2018; popular among lower-income Hindu and Muslim families
- **PMAY-G (Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana Gramin)** — central government rural housing scheme; in Nandigram, as across West Bengal, TMC panchayat workers were accused of demanding "cut money" (10–30% commission) from beneficiaries to process applications; this became a significant source of anti-incumbency
- **MGNREGA** — wage employment guarantee; similarly afflicted by cut-money complaints
- **Sabooj Sathi** — bicycles distributed to school students; popular and visible
- **Health insurance** — Mamata's state scheme covers state residents; BJP criticised the state government for blocking central **Ayushman Bharat** (Rs 5 lakh free health cover) rollout

### Cut-money backlash (2018–2019)

By mid-2019, public anger over **cut-money** (commissions extracted by TMC workers from government scheme beneficiaries) was sufficiently vocal that it became an issue in the Lok Sabha campaign. TMC leaders in some blocks — including East Midnapore — faced direct confrontations. Mamata Banerjee later publicly acknowledged the problem and asked party workers to return cut money. In Nandigram, this backlash contributed to some erosion of the TMC's previously unassailable margins, particularly among lower-income Hindu voters.

### CPM and INC: marginalised but present

- **CPI(M) / Left Front:** By 2019, reduced to a vestige in Nandigram. The firing of March 14, 2007 destroyed the CPI(M)'s moral legitimacy with the constituency's agrarian base. Former Left cadres had either migrated to TMC (accepting patronage) or nursed grievances in silence. Some CPI(M) holdouts — older schoolteachers, government employees, intellectuals — retained Left identity as a cultural badge, but electoral relevance was minimal.
- **Indian National Congress:** Similarly marginal; West Bengal Congress had not recovered from post-1977 decline.

## §6 BJP's Jangalmahal-adjacent appeal

### The 2019 surge and its anatomy

The BJP's near-37% vote share in Tamluk LS (2019) was part of a statewide wave driven by **Modi's personal popularity, Balakot nationalism, and Hindu consolidation**. In Nandigram's specific context, BJP inroads were driven by:

**Hindu consolidation:** By 2018–19, Hindutva outfits — Vishwa Hindu Parishad, Hindu Jagran Manch, RSS shakhas — had begun public activities across Purba Medinipur. Ram Navami processions became a visible assertive Hindu presence in the district. For younger Hindu men in both blocks of Nandigram, the BJP's Hindu-nationalist identity offered an alternative to what they perceived as TMC's cynical vote-bank politics centred on Muslim appeasement.

**CPI(M) voter migration:** In Nandigram II block and pockets of Nandigram I, former Left voters who had been tortured, humiliated, or economically marginalised by TMC's post-2011 dominance treated the BJP as the only viable anti-TMC vehicle. The BJP actively recruited former CPI(M) cadres in East Midnapore before the 2018 panchayat elections; in Nandigram, former Left karyakartas provided ground organisation for BJP that it lacked from within.

**Anti-corruption and welfare grievances:** The cut-money backlash (§5) translated some working-class and lower-middle-class Hindu anger into BJP support. The BJP's promise to deliver central schemes (Ayushman Bharat, PM Kisan) without the TMC intermediary layer resonated.

**SC fishermen communities:** Hindu SC households, including Kaibarta fishermen, had historically benefited from Left-era land reform and cooperative credit. Post-2011 TMC dominance offered new patrons but not necessarily better outcomes. BJP targeted SC communities through Scheduled Caste welfare rhetoric, particularly in coastal areas.

### The Muslim firewall

The BJP's gains in Nandigram were structurally capped by the **Muslim vote concentration** in Nandigram I block (~34%). In West Bengal's minority-dense constituencies, the Muslim vote behaved as a near-monolithic TMC bloc in 2019, largely because:
- The CAA/NRC prospect made the BJP appear existentially threatening
- Mamata's anti-CAA positioning was unambiguous and credible
- Memories of the 2007 agitation linked Muslim identity to TMC's founding narrative
- The Gazi and Sheikh communities of Nandigram I had well-established TMC linkages through booth networks

In a close election, Nandigram's Muslim voters would tip the balance decisively. This asymmetry — BJP consolidating Hindu votes, TMC retaining Muslim votes, with SC communities as swing actors — defines the structural electoral arithmetic.

### Geographic variation

BJP's 2019 gains were likely **stronger in Nandigram II block** (more homogenously Hindu, lower Muslim share, more interior) and **weaker in Nandigram I block** (Muslim concentration, proximity to 2007 martyrdom narrative). This geographic heterogeneity within the constituency means booth-level variation is high.

## §7 Media diet and information environment

### Television

The dominant Bengali-language news and entertainment ecosystem reaches Nandigram through:
- **Zee Bangla** and **Star Jalsha** — dominant entertainment channels; political talk shows on these channels shape discourse
- **ABP Ananda** — 24-hour Bengali news channel; agenda-setting for Bengali middle-class
- **Zee 24 Ghanta** and **News18 Bangla** — Bengali news; combined reach extends to rural households with DTH/cable
- **DD Bangla** — Doordarshan's state channel; still relevant in very rural and elderly demographics
- Local **cable TV operators** in Nandigram town and larger villages carry a mix of channels; DTH has expanded into rural Nandigram

Hindi news channels (Aaj Tak, Republic TV) have limited penetration in a non-Hindi-speaking area; Nandigram voters primarily consume news in **Bengali**.

### Print

- **Anandabazar Patrika** — dominant Bengali-language daily; read by literate middle-class and schoolteacher households
- **Ei Samay** — Kolkata Bengali daily, expanding readership; tends toward urban-professional audience
- **Bartaman** — popular Bengali daily with strong East Midnapore circulation; important for local politics
- **Ganashakti** — CPI(M)'s party newspaper; still read by Left holdouts but circulation has collapsed
- Local newspapers specific to the Tamluk/Haldia belt cover panchayat and block-level news that Kolkata papers miss

### Digital and WhatsApp

By end-2019, **WhatsApp** has become the dominant political communication channel at the village level, displacing earlier forms of poster/pamphlet politics:
- **Party WhatsApp groups** at booth and gram panchayat level circulate news, speeches, and rally calls
- **Rumour propagation** via WhatsApp is a significant vector for communal mobilisation; both BJP and TMC maintain groups
- **Low-quality video clips** of political speeches, real and doctored, circulate widely
- Smartphone penetration is moderate but growing in Nandigram's rural areas; feature phones with forwarded WhatsApp voice messages extend reach
- Facebook is secondary but used by politically active youth and local journalists
- Formal digital literacy is low; media discernment is limited

### Haldia FM radio

Community and commercial FM radio from Haldia reaches commuter workers in the Haldi corridor. This is primarily an entertainment medium but carries political advertising during elections.

### Information asymmetry

- Haldia-based industrial workers tend to be more media-literate and plugged into Bengali news channels
- Prawn farmers and fishermen in the coastal belt are more dependent on word-of-mouth, local cable, and WhatsApp
- Muslim households in Nandigram I, particularly older women, often receive political information filtered through mosque community networks and male family members rather than directly from mass media
- School-educated youth (the 18–30 cohort) are more likely to consume content from YouTube, Facebook, and Jio-enabled data streaming

## §8 Psychological archetypes

Below are eight voter personas representative of Nandigram's electorate as of end-2019. These are **ideal types** for simulation seeding — individual agents should vary around these archetypes.

### Persona A: The Muslim prawn farmer (Gazi/Sheikh community, Nandigram I block)

**Profile:** Male, 40–55, lives in a riverine village in Nandigram I. Cultivates paddy on 1.5 acres and manages a shrimp pond (bhery) on 0.5 acres leased from a landowner. Three children; eldest son commutes to Haldia for construction work. Has not left Nandigram in years. Remembers 2007 clearly — a relative was injured in the March 14 firing. Identifies as **bhumi putra**: this land is his family's for three generations.

**Political identity:** Strong TMC loyalist. Mamata Didi is his patron-protector; she stood with the farmers in 2007 when the Left tried to take their land. Suvendu-da's booth workers helped him get his PMAY application approved in 2017. CAA/NRC is a source of deep anxiety — he has heard stories from Assam about Muslim farmers declared "foreigners." Mamata's anti-CAA stand feels like personal protection.

**Vote calculus in 2019 Lok Sabha:** TMC, firmly. No consideration of BJP (existential threat). CPI(M) is the party that killed his relatives in 2007.

**Information environment:** WhatsApp from the local TMC booth group, ABP Ananda on evening television, mosque elders' commentary after Friday prayers.

**Uncertainty:** Mild dissatisfaction about prawn market collapse and infrastructure; low-level cut-money complaint about PMAY application process, but not actionable anger.

### Persona B: The Haldia HPCL/IOCL contract worker (Hindu, Nandigram I fringe)

**Profile:** Male, 28–40. Born in a Nandigram I village 8 km from Haldia. Works as a contract process operator at the Indian Oil refinery on a renewable 3-year contract through a manpower agency; earns Rs 14,000–18,000/month (well above agricultural incomes). Commutes daily by auto-rickshaw/ferry. His father was a CPI(M) CITU union member in the 1990s.

**Political identity:** Shifting. Grew up in a Left household; after 2011 shifted to TMC like most of his village. In 2019, voted for BJP in the Lok Sabha poll — drawn by Modi, Balakot, and a sense that TMC's corruption was corrosive. Watches Hindi and Bengali news; ABP Ananda and Republic Bangla on his phone.

### Constituency-level summary

As of end-2019, **Nandigram is a TMC stronghold under Suvendu Adhikari's stable management**, but with meaningful BJP penetration in its Hindu-majority southern block and among working-class Hindu voters statewide. The Muslim vote (~30%+ of the constituency) acts as a structural anchor for TMC. The 2007 wound is the constituency's foundational political myth — it delegitimises the Left and legitimises TMC simultaneously. Suvendu Adhikari's personal brand of boots-on-the-ground patronage politics holds the Hindu vote sufficiently to produce large TMC margins. BJP's trajectory is upward but has not yet threatened TMC's structural advantage in this seat.

Key uncertainty parameters for simulation:
- **Magnitude of Hindu consolidation** in Nandigram II block (higher uncertainty; BJP gains real but unmeasured at AC level)
- **Muslim CAA-anxiety intensity** — high as of December 2019; works to further entrench TMC Muslim vote
- **SC fishermen swing** — low salience but not negligible
- **Cut-money backlash translation** — some anti-incumbency priced in; question is whether it produced vote change or mere disgruntlement
