## 1. Who lives here

**AC 123 Sandeshkhali** sits at the northern fringe of the Sundarbans delta in
North 24 Parganas, Basirhat sub-division. The constituency is composed of all
8 Gram Panchayats of CD Block Sandeshkhali I and 7 of 8 GPs of CD Block
Sandeshkhali II (excluding Khulna GP). Total electorate ~2.3 lakh; 2019 turnout
86.1%.

The terrain is riverine and semi-tidal — a patchwork of river channels
(Raimangal, Haroa, Jamtola), low-lying paddy fields, bheri (prawn/fish ponds),
and mangrove fringes. There is no pucca road spine; inter-village movement often
relies on country boats (nauko) and narrow earthen embankment paths. Access to
Basirhat town — the nearest commercial hub — requires a boat leg plus a road
journey.

**Religion and community:** Hindu ~72% (2019 projection), Muslim ~27%.
The Hindu population is predominantly Scheduled Tribe (ST ~25% of AC total;
mainly Santhal, Munda, Oraon — descendants of 19th-century labour-settlement
waves brought to reclaim delta land) and Scheduled Caste (~37%, dominated by
Bagdi fishermen and Namasudra families). Upper-caste bhadralok presence is
negligible (~3%). Muslim households (~27%) are Bengali-Sheikh peasantry and
fishermen — overwhelmingly native, most with roots in the AC going back
generations; a small fraction has cross-border family ties (porous
India-Bangladesh tidal border ~30 km east).

The ST community's political identity is distinct: Santhal and Munda households
have their own cultural calendar (Sohrai, Karma, Baha festivals), their own
memory of displacement from the Chotanagpur plateau, and a long-standing
patron-client relationship with whoever controls the local panchayat. By 2019
that patron is TMC.

**Language:** Near-monolingual Bengali (~98%). Santhal/Munda households are
Bengali-primary but retain Sadri (Nagpuri) or Santali as a home language in
some families; this is not a media-consumption language — all political
communication is in Bengali.

## 2. The 2019 LS political shock

In the 2019 Lok Sabha election, Basirhat LS (PC 18) was retained by TMC but
the margin told a different story than 2014:

| CPI | Pallab Sengupta | 68,278 | 4.80 |
| Others / IND | various | 38,934 | 2.66 |

At the AC 123 segment level specifically, the BJP's performance was even
stronger relative to the LS aggregate: BJP took **38.83%** in Sandeshkhali vs
30.29% across the full Basirhat LS. TMC's 52.46% in the AC was weaker than its
54.93% LS-wide. This means Sandeshkhali's Hindu-majority, ST-heavy electorate
shifted toward BJP more than the LS average — driven partly by the
communal-polarization legacy of the 2017 Baduria–Basirhat riots and partly by
BJP's sharp anti-CAA/Muslim-infiltration messaging that landed harder in
riverine areas with a proximate Bangladesh border.

**Nusrat Jahan** was a TMC star candidate: a popular Bengali film actress and
Muslim woman, projecting secular glamour against BJP's polarization drive.
Her candidacy was Mamata Banerjee's deliberate signal that Basirhat's Muslim
plurality (INC's Quazi Abdur Rahim historically competed here on a minority-
solidarity ticket) should consolidate behind TMC. The INC's 7.32% LS-wide
(but only 1.43% in AC 123) shows that Muslim vote consolidation around Nusrat
largely succeeded in Sandeshkhali; INC's base partly survived in other LS
segments with stronger urban Muslim populations.

For the end-2019 persona: the election is six months past. Nusrat is the MP but
has limited visible constituency presence. The BJP's 38.83% in the AC has
rattled local TMC panchayat bosses — it is the highest BJP share Sandeshkhali
has ever seen. Local TMC cadres are more alert, more coercive, and more
interested in controlling the 2023 panchayat cycle than anything else.

## 3. The Basirhat communal riot aftermath — living memory of 2017

The single largest driver of Hindu voter movement toward BJP between 2014 and
2019 in this constituency is the **2017 Baduria–Basirhat communal riots**.

**What happened:** On 2 July 2017, a 17-year-old student in Baduria (AC 99,
also in Basirhat sub-division) shared a Facebook post deemed offensive to the
Prophet and the Kaaba. A crowd of an estimated 5,000 Muslim protesters blockaded
the Baduria police station demanding the boy's handover. Violence spread to
nearby Basirhat town: shops were torched, vehicles burned, one 65-year-old Hindu
man was stabbed and died. Sections of the Muslim crowd also targeted Hindu homes
and businesses. The police response was slow; Central Paramilitary Forces were
deployed only after the state appeared overwhelmed. Internet services were shut
across Basirhat sub-division for days.

**BJP's response:** The party deployed its national machinery to amplify
the riot. Fake images circulated on BJP-affiliated WhatsApp networks. National
BJP leaders called for President's Rule in Bengal. Kailash Vijayvargiya urged
Home Minister Rajnath Singh to intervene. The party launched a "Bistarak Yojana"
recruitment drive in Basirhat sub-division following the riots and claimed
10,000 fresh recruits within months, taking membership in the sub-division to
25,000. The BJP framed the TMC government as "appeasing Muslims" and letting
Hindus die.

**TMC's response:** Mamata Banerjee accused the BJP of spreading fake images
and inflaming the situation. TMC MLA Dipendu Biswas was removed as area
in-charge. Mamata clashed publicly with Governor Keshari Nath Tripathi, who
questioned the state's law-and-order response. TMC's position was that the
riots were BJP-incited, the immediate violence was contained, and communal peace
was Bengal's historical norm.

**Why Sandeshkhali specifically felt this:** Sandeshkhali is in the same
Basirhat sub-division — news of the Baduria–Basirhat violence reached
Sandeshkhali within hours via mobile and word of mouth. Hindu SC/ST households
in the AC heard that "Muslims attacked Hindu shops and a Hindu was killed in
Basirhat town." The BJP's narrative — "only we will protect Hindus" — gained
traction in Bagdi, Namasudra, and ST (Santhal/Munda) households in a way it
never had before 2017. This is the primary mechanism behind the BJP's 2019
jump from single-digits to 38.83% in this AC.

By end-2019, the riot is two-and-a-half years in the past, but it remains a
live scar. Hindu voters who swung to BJP in 2019 partly cite Baduria as
justification. Muslim voters in AC 123 feel the BJP's riot narrative unfairly
branded the entire Muslim community. The river-island geography means that in
several GP clusters, one community or the other has a strong majority — spatial
segregation by religion is moderate but real.

## 4. The CAA/NRC axis — Muslim community anxiety at end-2019

By end-2019, the Muslim ~27% of AC 123 is living through its most anxious
political moment in a generation.

**The sequence that landed hardest in Sandeshkhali:**

1. **Assam NRC — August 2019:** The final Assam NRC excluded 1.9 million
   people. Muslim families in Sandeshkhali followed this closely. Many have
   relatives — or share surnames — with Bengali Muslim families in Assam.
   The realization that a citizenship exercise could exclude long-settled Bengali
   Muslims (not just illegal migrants) created genuine fear. A Muslim fisherman
   whose family has lived in Sandeshkhali II for three generations has no birth
   certificate, no land title (fishing households often live on khas / vested
   land), and possibly an absent-father household due to out-migration. The
   Assam NRC made these documentation gaps feel existential.

2. **CAA passage — December 2019:** The Citizenship Amendment Act passed
   Parliament on December 11, 2019. It provides a citizenship path for
   non-Muslim refugees from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan who entered
   before December 31, 2014 — explicitly excluding Muslims. For Bengali Muslims
   in a borderland constituency adjacent to Bangladesh, the message was stark:
   if an NRC-style exercise comes to Bengal, non-Muslim undocumented residents
   will have a legal safety net; Muslims will not.

3. **Mamata Banerjee's counter-narrative:** The WB Assembly had already passed
   a resolution against NRC in September 2019. After CAA's passage, Mamata
   declared she would not allow NRC in Bengal and would not allow detention
   camps. Her "no NRC, no detention camp in Bengal" pledge is a direct and
   effective message to Muslim voters in Sandeshkhali. For many Muslim HHs, this
   is the single most important reason to keep voting TMC over INC or Left
   alternatives.

**Why the geography amplifies anxiety:** Sandeshkhali's tidal borderland
character — porous de facto boundary with Bangladesh, communities with
cross-border family ties, a fishing economy with minimal paper documentation —
makes the CAA/NRC axis feel more immediate than in mainland Bengali Muslim
communities. A Muslim crab collector who enters the Raimangal river estuary
with a forest-department pass has only that slip of paper between his livelihood
and the forest; he has no other formal documentation identity to offer if asked
to prove citizenship.

**Anti-CAA protests in Kolkata and elsewhere** (mid-December 2019) are
happening as this narrative snapshot is frozen. Local Muslim youth have heard
about the protests. Some have attended rallies in Basirhat town. But in
Sandeshkhali GP clusters, the dominant response is anxiety rather than street
protest — the geography isolates the constituency from urban activist networks.

## 5. The Sundarban economy — fishing, bheri, forest, and survival calculus

The livelihoods of Sandeshkhali's ~3.3 lakh residents cluster around four
interlocking activities, all shaped by the delta's ecological rhythms and
increasingly by its ecological fragility.

### 5a. Fishing and crab collection

Open-water fishing in the tidal river channels and the Bay of Bengal mouth —
rohu, hilsa, bhetki, tiger prawn — is the oldest livelihood. Fishermen go out
in groups in country boats (nauko) or motor-fitted fibre boats for 3–7 day
stints. Crab collection in the mangrove channels is a secondary livelihood,
practiced by both Hindu (Bagdi) and Muslim (Sheikh) fishermen. The catch is
sold at local landing ghats and transported in ice-boxes to Basirhat's fish
market or onward to Kolkata.

The fishing economy is male-dominated but women participate in cleaning,
sorting, and drying. Seasonal income is highly volatile — monsoon fishing bans
(June–August mandated by Fisheries Dept.), cyclone shutdowns, and river
erosion of landing ghats all reduce monthly income unpredictably.

### 5b. Bheri (prawn and fish aquaculture)

The most transformative economic shift over the 1990s–2010s has been the
conversion of paddy land to bheri — brackish-water ponds for tiger shrimp
(bagda) and galda prawn cultivation. Bheris require minimal labour input but
significant capital (pond repair, fingerling procurement, feed). By 2019 in
Sandeshkhali, a small stratum of bheri owners controls substantial economic
and political leverage. The majority of the population is bheri *labour* —
daily-wage workers maintaining embankments, harvesting catch — not owners.

The ecological tradeoff is stark: once converted to brackish bheri, the land
cannot easily return to paddy cultivation because saltwater infiltration
permanently degrades soil quality. Families who sold or leased paddy land for
bheri conversion in the 1990s are now fully dependent on bheri wage labour or
fishing, with no food-production fallback.

By 2019, the local TMC strongman network (illustrated later in section 6) has
inserted itself deeply into bheri supply chains: controlling fingerling supply,
fish market access, and the land parcels on which bheris operate. This creates
a political-economic dependency that reinforces booth-level TMC control.

### 5c. Forest-based livelihoods (honey, firewood, NTFP)

A significant minority of households — predominantly ST (Santhal, Munda) and
some SC Bagdi households — depend on Non-Timber Forest Products (NTFPs) from
the Sundarbans mangrove reserve: moule (honey) collection in the
March–May season, golpata (nipa palm fronds) for thatching, firewood, and
seasonal prawn-seed (meen) collection in river mouths.

Every NTFP collector must obtain a permit (pass) from the Forest Department
before entering reserve forest compartments. This creates a dependency on
the Block Forest Office and, by extension, on local political intermediaries
who can smooth the permit process. The permit regime is also a patronage tool:
TMC-aligned panchayat pradhans and block-level functionaries are seen as
gatekeepers to forest livelihoods.

**Tiger risk as constant backdrop:** Approximately 40 human fatalities per year
occur across the Indian Sundarbans from tiger attacks. Fishermen, crab
collectors, honey collectors, and woodcutters are the primary victims. Men
absorbed in work in riverine channels or dense mangrove forest are stalked from
behind. Sandeshkhali households in the blocks bordering the Sundarbans reserve
(Sandeshkhali I more than II) have direct mortality exposure. "Tiger widow"
households — where the male earner was killed — face acute poverty and social
stigma. As of end-2019 this is not a political issue per se, but it shapes
the psychology of risk-tolerance and fatalism in forest-adjacent communities.

## 6. TMC dominance and the state welfare stack

### 6a. Panchayat control

TMC won the 2018 Panchayat elections in North 24 Parganas in circumstances
widely reported as coercive — opposition candidates unable to file nominations
in many GPs, booths without opposition polling agents, incidents of violence
during counting. In Sandeshkhali, both CDB-I and CDB-II GPs are under TMC
panchayat control by 2019. The pradhan, upa-pradhan, and zilla parishad
representatives are TMC or TMC-aligned.

A local TMC strongman (who by 2013 had switched from CPM to TMC, and by 2019
had become a key organizer credited with TMC's LS booth management in
Sandeshkhali) operates through control of bheri supply chains and fish markets.
His political role in the 2019 LS election — mobilizing booth-level
organisation, overseeing vote delivery — has already made him a figure of
local clout. His name is familiar to Sandeshkhali voters; in 2019 he is not
yet a prominent public controversy but is recognized within the AC as "the
man who runs things." [Note to simulator: Do NOT reference his arrest, any
2024 allegations, or the national controversy that followed — these are
strictly post-2019.]

### 6b. Welfare schemes as lived experience

By end-2019, TMC's welfare stack is the texture of daily life for most
households in AC 123:

- **Khadya Sathi** (PDS subsidy, 2016): ~90% HH coverage in the AC; Rs 2/kg
  rice is the baseline food security for BPL and near-BPL families. A family
  without a ration card goes to the panchayat; the panchayat is TMC.
- **Kanyashree** (girl-student stipend, 2013): Rs 750/year for 13–18 year old
  girls in school, Rs 25,000 at 18 if unmarried and enrolled. Widely credited
  by mothers as a reason to keep daughters in school. In an AC with 25% female
  illiteracy (older cohorts), this scheme has genuine uptake.
- **Swasthya Sathi** (health insurance, 2016): Rs 5 lakh per family per year.
  Coverage expanded to all WB residents by 2019. For a fishing or bheri-labour
  family with no savings, a hospital stay that would have meant selling land
  can now be covered. This is the scheme that generates the strongest emotional
  loyalty among middle-aged women in the AC.
- **Sabuj Sathi** (bicycle, 2015): Free bicycles for Class 9–12 students. In an
  AC without paved roads, a bicycle is genuinely transformative for girls' school
  attendance — the bicycle travels the embankment paths that boats cannot.
- **Krishak Bandhu** (farmer support, January 2019): Rs 5,000/acre/year in two
  installments + Rs 2 lakh death benefit for farming HHs. Launched just before
  the LS election. Bheri-operator and paddy-cultivator households enrolled;
  for a small cultivator with 2 acres this is Rs 10,000/year — real money in a
  subsistence economy.
- **Bhumi-Prapta / ST land patta (Forest Rights Act):** Distribution of
  homestead and cultivation pattas (land titles) to ST households under the
  Forest Rights Act 2006 has been an ongoing TMC process through the 2010s.
  For Santhal and Munda families on khas or vested land with no formal title,
  a patta from the state government creates tangible material security and
  a political credit to TMC.

**Note:** Lakshmir Bhandar does not exist in 2019. No monthly cash transfer
to women exists yet. Do not confuse welfare sentiment with Lakshmir Bhandar
familiarity.

### 6c. The TMC booth machine

In a constituency with 240–250 polling stations, many reachable only by boat,
TMC's organisational depth is its electoral moat. Each booth has a TMC agent
who knows every voter family, tracks which HHs received which scheme benefits,
and coordinates transport on polling day. The 2019 BJP surge to 38.83% happened
despite this machine — not because of any weakness in TMC's booth infrastructure,
but because enough Hindu voters chose to vote against TMC even at social cost
(TMC retaliation at panchayat level against opposition-voting HHs is a
recognised dynamic in this AC).

## 7. Cyclone geography — Aila memory and Bulbul fresh wound

### 7a. Aila 2009 — the foundational trauma

Cyclone Aila (May 25, 2009) struck both Sandeshkhali I and II blocks with
catastrophic force. Storm surges breached embankments across the sub-division;
paddy fields flooded with saline water; shrimp ghers were inundated; thatched
houses collapsed across all 15 GPs. Hundreds of thousands of people were
displaced to embankment-top camps. An estimated 5–8% of Sandeshkhali households
migrated semi-permanently toward the Kolkata periphery and never returned; those
who stayed rebuilt on the same flood-prone plots.

The Aila memory shapes how Sandeshkhali voters think about state capacity:
the Left Front government (in power in 2009) was perceived as slow, and the
initial TMC response (in opposition federally at the time, Mamata aggressively
doing constituency work in Sundarban areas) is credited by many as the moment
TMC won over Sundarbans voters. By 2011 TMC swept North 24 Parganas. That
"Mamata was here when Aila hit" narrative still circulates in 2019.

### 7b. Cyclone Bulbul — November 2019, a fresh wound

On November 9–10, 2019, Cyclone Bulbul made landfall at the Sundarbans coast
(Sagar Island, South 24 Parganas) with wind speeds of ~135 km/h. Though the
main landfall was slightly south, North 24 Parganas took a severe hit. Reports
from the ground explicitly noted that **the condition of Sandeshkhali was "very
bad"** in the immediate aftermath.

Damage across the Sundarbans region included:
- Paddy and winter crops flattened — aman paddy was just weeks from harvest
- Betel-leaf orchards destroyed
- Bheri embankments breached, stocking fish and prawns lost
- Mud-and-thatch houses collapsed in the tens of thousands
- Electric poles snapped, grid power out for days to weeks

**State response:** Mamata declared an aerial survey and announced relief,
deploying NDRF teams. In the immediate post-cyclone period, TMC panchayats
controlled relief distribution — ration kits, tarpaulins, cash relief. This
is a double-edged dynamic in the local political economy: TMC gets credit for
relief delivery but is also the gatekeeper of who gets what, reinforcing the
political dependency described in Section 6.

For end-2019 personas: Bulbul struck only ~6 weeks before this narrative
snapshot. Fishing families are still repairing boats. Bheri owners are
assessing crop losses. Paddy-farming families have had their aman harvest
partly or fully destroyed. The acute cyclone-recovery phase is still ongoing.
It is the loudest immediate concern for most households in the AC, cutting
across religion and caste. A persona generated as of end-2019 should carry
the Bulbul experience as fresh memory — the kind that still generates anger,
grief, or relief-negotiation anxiety.

## 8. Out-migration dynamics

Male out-migration from Sandeshkhali is structurally driven by the mismatch
between available delta livelihoods and the number of working-age men. Key
streams:

- **Construction labour (Kolkata and peripheral towns):** The largest category.
  Men in their 20s–40s go to Kolkata's construction sites, New Town/Rajarhat
  development zones, or Bongaon-Habra suburban expansion. 8–10 months away per
  year; Rs 400–600/day vs Rs 150–200/day in local agriculture. Remittances are
  the cash backbone of many HHs.
- **Fishing expeditions (deeper sea):** Some men join mechanized trawler crews
  for the deep-sea shrimp catch off Digha/Sagar/Kakdwip — sometimes for 15–25
  day stints. More dangerous, higher income. Sea-fishing crews are predominantly
  Muslim and Hindu Bagdi; ST households are less represented here.
- **Brick kilns (Nadia, Murshidabad, Bihar border):** Seasonal; some Santhal
  and Munda men join migratory brick-kiln labour gangs after harvest.

The political consequence of male out-migration is that **women in Sandeshkhali
manage daily household politics** — ration card, Kanyashree applications,
panchayat interactions, relief claims. Female voters in this AC are often more
directly in contact with TMC's welfare delivery machinery than their husbands.
This partially explains the TMC female-voter advantage visible in D.17 of the
calibrated profile.

A second consequence: out-migrant men who return for elections bring urban
media exposure and, in some cases, BJP WhatsApp group material circulated on
construction sites in outer Kolkata (where BJP is more visible than in rural
Sandeshkhali). This is one channel through which the BJP vote inflated in 2019
among Hindu male voters.

## 9. Media diet

- **Bengali print (low reach):** Anandabazar Patrika and Bartaman have limited
  rural penetration in Sandeshkhali — newspaper delivery is unreliable in
  river-access-only GP clusters. Some GPs get newspapers a day late. A subset
  of educated households reads newspapers; most do not.
- **Bengali TV (primary medium):** ABP Ananda and Zee 24 Ghanta are the
  dominant Bengali news channels, reaching ~60% of HHs via cable or DTH
  (television ownership ~60% per calibrated profile). TV news is the primary
  political information channel for literate adults. ABP Ananda is seen as
  more balanced; Zee 24 Ghanta as somewhat more sensation-oriented. Both are
  in Bengali and cover Bengal politics as their primary beat.
- **Radio:** ~8% of HHs own a radio — proportionally higher than mainland ACs
  because radio is more resilient to grid outages and useful during cyclone
  emergency. AIR Kolkata covers this region. Relevant during disasters.
- **WhatsApp:** Smartphone penetration ~32% in AC 123 (Jio rollout 2016–19 has
  reached many delta areas, but connectivity is patchy). WhatsApp is the
  primary political misinformation vector — BJP-affiliated groups (nationally)
  and TMC booth-level groups both operate here. Fake images of the 2017 Baduria
  riots circulated via WhatsApp first; local voters recall this. Out-migrant
  construction workers bring smartphone usage habits back home.
- **Political mobilization media:** TMC runs party-level audio systems at
  rallies; boat-based loudspeaker campaigns through GP channels are an older
  form of local political communication that still functions in river areas
  inaccessible to road-based campaigns.
- **Hindu nationalist media:** Organiser and Panchjanya have negligible direct
  reach in this AC. BJP's polarization messaging reaches delta voters mainly
  through WhatsApp forwards and returned out-migrants.

1. **The Santhal elder (65+, male, CDB-I, ST):** Born in Sandeshkhali, family

3. **The Bagdi SC housewife (40s, female, CDB-II, Hindu SC):** Husband is a

4. **The bheri-labourer youth (22, male, Hindu SC, CDB-I):** Class 10 passed,

5. **The panchayat-dependent cultivator widow (55, female, Hindu ST, CDB-II):**

   relief. The BJP is "bahari lok" (outsiders) to her — she knows no BJP

6. **The educated Muslim young woman (20, female, Muslim, CDB-I):** BA first

7. **The Hindu middle-aged trader (45, male, OBC Mahishya, CDB-II, small

## Appendix — what is deliberately NOT in this file

A persona frozen at end-2019, even one living in Sandeshkhali, does not yet know:

- That **Cyclone Amphan** (May 2020) will devastate the Sundarbans, breaching
  hundreds of embankments and causing destruction far exceeding Bulbul — the
  worst cyclone in recorded Bengal history. A persona thinking "Bulbul was bad"
  does not know worse is six months away.
- That **Lakshmir Bhandar** (Rs 500/1000 monthly cash to women) will be announced
  in February 2021 and launched in August 2021. No monthly cash transfer to
  women exists as of end-2019.
- That **Sheikh Shahjahan**, the local TMC organiser credited with the 2019 LS
  booth mobilisation, will become the center of a nationally prominent land-grab
  and sexual-assault controversy in January–February 2024, leading to his arrest
  and TMC's suspension of him from all positions. In 2019 he is simply the
  most powerful local TMC figure — a familiar presence, not a national scandal.
- That the **2021 WB Assembly Election** result for AC 123 will be: TMC's
  Sukumar Mahata 54.64%, BJP's Bhaskar Sardar 35.36% — a TMC consolidation
  relative to 2019's 52.46%.
- That the **2024 LS result** for AC 123 will flip to BJP (47.46% BJP, 43.31%
  TMC) in the specific wake of the Shahjahan controversy, making Sandeshkhali
  briefly one of the most watched constituencies in India.
- That **CAA rules** will go unnotified until March 2024 — the law exists as
  of December 2019 but has no implementation mechanism yet.
- That **COVID-19** will arrive in Bengal in March 2020 and restructure
  every livelihood calculation in the AC.
- That **river erosion** will accelerate in subsequent years, further shrinking
  cultivable land in the delta blocks.
- That any post-2019 communal incident, political defection, or panchayat
  restructuring will occur.

If your persona's psychology depends on knowing any of the above, the persona
is not 2019-frozen — revise before running the narrative engine.
