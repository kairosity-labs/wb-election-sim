## 1. Who lives here

**AC 198 Tarakeswar** sits in Hooghly district, in the Chandannagore subdivision.
The constituency spans Tarakeswar Municipality (the urban temple-town core,
~10% of population), the full 10 GPs of Tarakeswar CD Block (~60%), and 5 GPs
of Dhaniakhali CD Block (~30%). Total electorate ~2.32 lakh in 2019; turnout
84.83% — one of the higher-participation seats in the pilot sample.

The population is majority Bengali Hindu (~87%), with a Muslim minority (~12%,
concentrated in the Dhaniakhali fringe GPs) and a small ST population (~7%,
mostly Santal, also in the Dhaniakhali fringe). The dominant Hindu cultivating
castes are Mahishya (~18% of total population, the single largest caste group),
Sadgop (~6%), and Kurmi/Tili (~6% combined). Scheduled Castes are ~25.5%,
led by Bagdi (~9.5%) and Bauri (~4.5%). Upper-caste bhadralok (Brahmin,
Kayastha, Baidya) make up ~8%, concentrated in the municipality, with temple
priests (Brahmin) a visible occupational sub-group. The Muslim population is
overwhelmingly Bengali-Sheikh peasantry, native to the district for generations
— not a refugee or migration story.

The constituency is **semi-rural and semi-urban**: it is close enough to Kolkata
(58 km by rail) to sustain a large daily-commuter workforce, yet agricultural
enough that paddy, vegetables, and marigolds for the Kolkata market define the
working calendar for a majority of households. It is not a border-district
constituency; the dominant anxieties here are economic rather than citizenship.

## 2. The 2019 LS political shock

AC 198 Tarakeswar lies within **Arambagh Lok Sabha constituency (PC 29, SC
reserved)**. In 2019 the Arambagh result was the **closest in West Bengal** —
arguably the closest in the country:

  - **Aparupa Poddar / Afrin Ali (AITC)**: 6,49,929 votes — 44.14%, **WON**
  - **Tapan Kumar Roy (BJP)**: 6,48,787 votes — 44.06%, lost by **1,142 votes**
  - **Sakti Mohan Malik (CPI(M))**: ~8%, a shadow of what the Left once commanded
  - **INC**: ~1.7%; Others/NOTA: ~2.1%

Within AC 198 specifically (ECI segment data):

  - AITC: 88,096 votes — **44.67%**
  - BJP: 83,753 votes — **42.47%**
  - CPI(M): 15,747 — **7.99%**
  - NOTA: 2,938 — **1.49%**
  - AITC margin over BJP: **4,343 votes (2.20 pp)**

This is a true swing seat — TMC won narrowly, BJP is right at the door. For
comparison, in 2009 Arambagh was a CPI(M) "Red Fort" won by a margin of
~2,01,558 votes. By 2014 it had flipped to TMC (+3.5 lakh margin). By 2019 BJP
had come from near-zero to within 1,142 votes of flipping it. The psychological
shock in end-2019: Tarakeswar is no longer safe for anyone.

State-wide context: BJP won 18 of 42 LS seats in Bengal in 2019 (up from 2);
TMC held 22 (down from 34); Left won 0 (down from 2). The "Bengal is becoming
a real two-party state" narrative entered the bloodstream. An end-2019 voter in
Tarakeswar has absorbed this shock.

## 3. Left decline and the TMC–BJP contest

Tarakeswar's political history is the story of the Left Front's long dominance
and abrupt collapse. The seat was held by the Marxist Forward Bloc continuously
from the Left Front's rise to power (1977) through 2011 — Pratim Chatterjee
won in 2001 and 2006 as an Independent with MFB support. That is **34 years**
of unbroken Left rule. For voters over 50 in this constituency, CPI(M) or MFB
was the air they breathed politically.

The 2011 AITC wave swept it decisively: Rachhpal Singh (AITC) won with a
25,472-vote margin. He retained it in 2016 with a 27,690 margin. Rachhpal Singh
served as Minister for Planning in the WB government — a meaningful local
patronage connection. The constituency's sitting MLA through the 2019 LS
election is Rachhpal Singh.

By 2019 the CPI(M) has collapsed to ~8% as a stable bystander, not a contender.
Its voters have split two ways: older peasant households that felt Left welfare
patronage (operation-barga land-reform beneficiaries, panchayat-level workers)
moved toward TMC; younger, anti-incumbent, Hindu-identity-activated voters
moved toward BJP. The "swing voter" in 2019 is not a Left-to-TMC convert — that
happened in 2011. The swing in 2019 is about whether a Hindu OBC cultivator
household that voted TMC in 2011 and 2016 will defect to BJP over a combination
of: anti-Mamata fatigue, Modi's national persona, and BJP's cultural-Hindu pitch.

Arambagh Lok Sabha was historically "CPI(M)'s Red Fort." In 2009 CPI(M) won it
by 2,01,558 votes. By 2019 CPI(M) got ~8%. This collapse is **the dominant
structural fact** of contemporary Hooghly politics. For a 60-year-old Bagdi
agricultural labourer who voted Left from 1977 to 2006, this is a world turned
upside down.

## 4. The pilgrimage economy — Tarakeswar Shiva temple

The Taraknath Temple is the economic and spiritual axis of the constituency.
Built in 1729 by Raja Bharamalla Rao, it is one of Bengal's most important Shiva
shrines — a distinctive "atchala" structure with a natmandir in front. Pilgrims
visit year-round, especially on Mondays (Shivratri-adjacent devotional practice),
but the peak season is the **Shravani Mela** (Shraban/Sawan, July–August).

**Scale of the Shravani Mela:** Approximately **10 million devotees** annually
make the yatra during the Sawan month. Pilgrims — called "Jal Jatris" — walk
roughly 39–40 km barefoot from Nimai Tirtha Ghat at Baidyabati, carrying holy
Ganga water to offer to Lord Taraknath. This is cited as the **largest mela in
West Bengal**. The GoWB has a dedicated Tarakeswar Shravani Mela committee and
official website (tarakeswarshrabanimela.wb.gov.in).

**Economic footprint of the temple economy:**
  - Hotels, lodges, ashrams, and dharamshalas ring the temple town. At least a
    dozen lodging options operate year-round (WBTDC tourist lodge, private
    ashoke nibas, family-run guest houses, niramish hotels — vegetarian-only
    accommodation for pilgrims).
  - Flower vendors: marigold garlands, bel patra (bilva leaves), flowers for
    abhishek — a permanent daily trade connecting local cultivators to the
    temple gate economy.
  - Prasad traders, sweet-makers, conch-shell and brassware shops, religious
    goods (rudraksha, Shiva-linga replicas, sacred thread, incense).
  - Tea stalls, food stalls, transport-touts, auto-rickshaw operators.
  - During Sawan, temporary markets mushroom: handicrafts, local sweets, clay
    figurines, temporary accommodation.

The municipality's own statement: *"The economy of the town is based mainly on
Agriculture and Trading, very small in Industries. Tourism is another sector from
where income is derived, and due to the presence of Tarakeswar temple … the town
has several hotels and restaurants."*

**Political relevance:** The temple economy has two faces. It sustains a visible
bhadralok-Brahmin priestly class and a Tili/trader community — both somewhat
BJP-adjacent by 2019 (culturally Hindu, commercially urban, part of the national
Hindu revivalism BJP invokes). But the temple also sits comfortably within
TMC's administration of the municipality; Rachhpal Singh's government has
managed the mela infrastructure. For a pilgrim-economy trader, the question is:
does he trust BJP's Hindu pride appeal over TMC's local patronage delivery?

## 5. The agrarian economy — paddy, vegetables, brick kilns

The majority of the electorate's working households live from agriculture. The
calibrated workforce profile: ~18% cultivators, ~40% agricultural labourers,
among working adults.

**Paddy:** The Tarakeswar CDB is a rice-belt area in Hooghly's cultivating zone.
Two paddy cycles (Aman/kharif — harvested Nov–Dec; Boro/rabi — harvested
Apr–May) are the primary agricultural calendar. Landholdings are mostly small
(below 2 acres) and fragmented — a typical Mahishya or Sadgop cultivator has
2–5 bigha of family land, usually partly sharecropped.

**Vegetables and flowers:** With Kolkata market access (Howrah–Tarakeswar rail),
a subset of cultivators grow vegetables (potatoes, green vegetables) and flowers
(marigolds, tuberose/rajanigandha) as cash crops for the Kolkata market and for
the temple-gate daily flower trade in Tarakeswar town. Flower cultivation is a
growing niche: economically more efficient per acre than paddy when market
connections hold. The temple provides a guaranteed local buyer; Kolkata's flower
market at Mullick Ghat absorbs regional supply. Agricultural produce (potatoes,
green vegetables) also moves to Kolkata daily via rail, per station records at
Tarakeswar junction.

**Brick kilns:** Hooghly district is one of WB's major brickfield belts —
the hinterland of Kolkata (Hooghly, Howrah, North and South 24 Parganas)
supplies the city's construction economy. West Bengal has approximately 3,500
brick-manufacturing units employing over 700,000 people from the rural
agricultural sector. Some of these brickfields sit within or near the
constituency. For SC labourers (Bagdi, Bauri) and marginal ST workers
(Santal from the Dhaniakhali fringe), brickfield employment is a major
livelihood option during agricultural off-season. Work typically runs 8 months
a year. Labour is partly local; partly migrant from Jharkhand and other states
who camp near the kiln site for the season. Local Bagdi/Bauri workers earn daily
wages at brickfields — cash income in an otherwise barter-adjacent agricultural
economy. Working conditions are poor; advance-loan bondage is documented; the
'pathera' (brick-moulder), 'matikata' (earth-digger), 'taboya' (basket-bearer),
and 'reja' (furnace-carrier) roles are all occupied by the poorest manual workers.

**PM Kisan non-receipt:** The WB government refused to participate in the central
PM-Kisan scheme (launched December 2018, ₹6,000/yr for cultivators). **No
Tarakeswar cultivator received a single PM-Kisan rupee in 2019.** BJP used this
as a campaign grievance: "Mamata is blocking your money from Delhi." Among
cultivator households aware of PM-Kisan, this directionally favoured BJP by
perhaps 5pp in the 2019 LS (see calibrated profile D.18).

**Krishak Bandhu:** TMC's counter was Krishak Bandhu, launched January 2019
just before the LS campaign. First installment (Kharif, ₹5,000/acre for
cultivators with ≥1 acre) was delivered May 2019, just before the May 6 poll.
For ~18% of AC households who are cultivators, this was a real pre-election
cash deposit. For landless labourers, it provided no direct benefit — but the
scheme's death benefit (₹2 lakh to cultivator family on death) was an insurance
component that TMC cadres communicated widely.

## 6. The daily commuter economy — Hooghly industrial belt and Kolkata

The **Howrah–Sheoraphuli–Tarakeswar branch line** (electrified 1957–58) is the
economic lifeline of the constituency. Tarakeswar railway station is described
as "one of the most busy railway stations with high revenue of Eastern Railway"
in the suburban network. The line connects Tarakeswar to Sheoraphuli (~20 min),
Howrah (~55 km, ~1.5 hours on local), and Kolkata central.

**Daily commuter workforce:** Estimates suggest approximately 32 lakh people
commute to Kolkata from the Tarakeswar rail catchment. At the AC-level, a
substantial fraction of the ~25% services+manufacturing+transport workforce
(calibrated profile C.8) is composed of daily commuters: private-sector clerks,
traders, transport workers, informal service workers in Kolkata/Howrah. These
households have one foot in the village (family home, agricultural land,
Khadya Sathi card) and one foot in the city (Kolkata employer, urban media
exposure, smartphone). They are higher-information voters than pure agricultural
households.

**Hooghly industrial belt history:** The district was Bengal's jute-mill hub.
Wellington jute mill in Hooghly district (est. 1856) was one of the first jute
mills in India; Hastings, Angus, and Victoria jute mills followed. At peak, the
industry drew lakhs of workers. By 2019, most large jute mills have closed or
run at a fraction of capacity. The industry "lies in shambles" per journalistic
accounts; workers from Bihar, Odisha, and UP who once flooded in have returned
home. What remains is the memory of industrial employment — the idea that factory
work was once available here — alongside a residual engineering and logistics
small-industry footprint in areas like Chandannagore and Srirampur in the broader
district. For older voters in the CDB who grew up expecting mill employment, the
loss of the industrial economy is a generational grievance with no clear partisan
target (Left presided over much of the decline; TMC hasn't reversed it; BJP
offers aspiration without specifics).

**Commuter political identity:** A daily-train commuter to Kolkata is exposed to
Bengali print media (Anandabazar Patrika, Bartaman, Ei Samay in the morning
train), WhatsApp forwards on the phone, and Bengali news TV in the evening. By
2019, smartphone penetration in this commuter workforce is ~75% (calibrated
profile D.8). They are the most media-literate segment of the AC's electorate.
Their concerns: employment in Kolkata's informal sector, train fares, railway
station infrastructure, petty corruption at the town level, and the national
political drama they consume on their phones. They are more BJP-reachable than
pure agricultural households — but not solidly in any camp.

## 7. The TMC welfare stack in a semi-rural semi-industrial constituency

The Mamata Banerjee government's welfare architecture, by end-2019, has five
main contact points with Tarakeswar households:

  - **Khadya Sathi** (Jan 2016) — subsidized PDS rice (~₹2/kg). Near-universal
    (~90% WB population). A Bagdi agricultural labourer's family and a Mahishya
    cultivator family both have a Khadya Sathi card — this is the floor of the
    welfare relationship.

  - **Swasthya Sathi** (Dec 2016; expanded 2019) — cashless health insurance
    up to ₹5 lakh/year for the whole family. The 2019 expansion to "all WB
    residents" was a campaign-season announcement. For a rural household that
    cannot afford private hospital bills, this is real. Calibrated profile D.18:
    ~50% TMC among Swasthya Sathi-exposed households (vs ~38% BJP).

  - **Kanyashree** (2013) — ₹750/yr stipend for girls 13–18 in school + ₹25,000
    one-time at 18 if unmarried and in education. ~80 lakh girl students enrolled
    WB-wide by 2019. A rural mother in Tarakeswar whose daughter is in Class 9
    is a Kanyashree mother. This is the strongest direct-transfer connection
    between TMC and women voters. CSDS 2019 WB: ~50% women voted TMC vs ~38%
    BJP — the gender gap is partly this scheme.

  - **Sabuj Sathi** (2015) — free bicycles to Class 9–12 students. ~1 crore
    distributed WB-wide by 2019. In a semi-rural constituency with limited
    local transport, a bicycle for a rural girl to reach her school is a
    tangible material object that a household associates with "Didi."

  - **Krishak Bandhu** (Jan 2019, just before the LS campaign) — ₹5,000/acre
    (in two installments) + ₹2 lakh death benefit for registered cultivators.
    For a 2-acre Mahishya cultivator household: ₹10,000/year in direct transfers,
    arriving twice annually. First Kharif installment: May 2019 — literally
    just before the May 6 Arambagh poll. This was TMC's most targeted election-
    season welfare intervention. Among cultivator households (~18% of the AC),
    Krishak Bandhu is reported to suppress BJP vote by ~5pp compared to non-
    cultivator households.

**What is absent in 2019:** Lakshmir Bhandar (₹500–1000/month direct cash for
women, announced Feb 2021, launched Aug 2021) does NOT exist. This is
critical — the strongest single pro-TMC welfare lever that the 2021 AE
analysis would reveal is simply not in this world. A 2019 persona attributing
her vote to monthly cash from the government is wrong.

**BJP's welfare counter-narrative:** PM-Kisan (₹6,000/year, launched Dec 2018)
was blocked by WB government. BJP candidates in Tarakeswar and Arambagh
campaigned with the explicit line: "Your ₹6,000 from Modi is being kept from
you by Mamata." For cultivators aware of PM-Kisan through TV/WhatsApp, this
was a real grievance. TMC's counter: "Krishak Bandhu gives you more" (₹10,000
for ≥1 acre). The crop of voters in the 1-3 acre range are the contested
battlefield.

## 8. Media diet

  - **Bengali print (morning train read):** Anandabazar Patrika (dominant;
    moderate, pro-establishment), Bartaman (TMC-friendly), Ei Samay (Kolkata-
    centric, politically centrist), Pratidin. A commuter reads ABP or Bartaman
    on the Howrah local.

  - **Bengali TV (evening, household):** ABP Ananda (most-watched Bengali news
    channel in 2019), Zee 24 Ghanta, News18 Bangla. These three channels run
    24-hour state political coverage — the Arambagh nail-biter result was a
    major story on all three. TV penetration ~76% AC-wide (higher in
    municipality, lower in Dhaniakhali fringe GPs).

  - **National TV:** Doordarshan (older households), Republic TV (Arnab Goswami;
    aggressive Modi-support; some viewership among Hindi-speaking traders and
    educated men who follow national politics). Republic Bangla does NOT yet
    exist in 2019 (launched 2021 — out of scope).

  - **WhatsApp:** Critical and growing. Smartphone penetration ~45–85% depending
    on sub-unit (municipality vs rural CDB). Commuters are heavy WhatsApp
    users. BJP's WhatsApp network in Hooghly was one of the most organised in
    WB in 2019 — Locket Chatterjee's campaign in the neighbouring Hooghly LS
    seat was partly WhatsApp-driven. TMC has booth-level WhatsApp groups.
    Religious content (Jai Shri Ram, Shiva devotional content) blends into
    political content for many users who don't cleanly separate them.

  - **Pilgrimage-season media:** During Shravan, Kolkata TV and regional channels
    run Taraknath-temple devotional content. The temple's media identity
    reinforces the Hindu-cultural salience of the constituency in the statewide
    imagination.

  - **Language note:** ~92% Bengali-speaking; ~3.5% Hindi (mainly temple-town
    traders and pilgrim-economy settlers). The Hindi-speaking trader fringe in
    Tarakeswar municipality has access to Hindi national TV (Aaj Tak, ABP News,
    Republic TV). This is a small but politically BJP-aligned segment.

## 9. Common psychological archetypes (6–8 personas)

Use these as starting templates — vary, blend, and complicate them with the
demographic axes the persona carries.

  - **The Mahishya cultivator patriarch (55+, male, Tarakeswar CDB, 3–4 bigha
    land, Krishak Bandhu recipient):** Voted CPI(M) in 1991, TMC in 2011 and
    2016, genuinely undecided in 2019. Krishak Bandhu gave him ₹5,000 in May
    2019, just before the poll — real money in his account. But his neighbour
    says Delhi would give him ₹6,000 if only Mamata let him. He watches ABP
    Ananda; his son has a smartphone with BJP WhatsApp forwards. He values land
    security over cultural politics; the Shiva temple is personal faith, not a
    political marker. In May 2019 he voted AITC, barely.

  - **The Bagdi agricultural labourer (40s, male, Tarakeswar CDB, SC):**
    Landless or near-landless. Works paddy-harvesting in season; brickfield
    labour in off-season (6–8 months at a kiln). Khadya Sathi card is his
    household's monthly security. A family member got Swasthya Sathi treatment
    once. Has no Krishak Bandhu — he doesn't own land. Feels the Left's collapse
    personally: his father was a khet-majur who voted CPI(M) for 30 years; the
    gram panchayat gave the family some leverage. TMC replaced that; it's
    imperfect but real. BJP is abstract — "Delhi people." Voted AITC without
    much deliberation.

  - **The Tarakeswar municipality temple-economy trader (40s, male, UC Brahmin
    or Tili, town):** Runs a prasad shop or a pilgrim-goods store near the
    Taraknath temple. Business depends on footfall from the Sawan mela —
    roughly 2 months of serious income, rest of year is moderate. Educated to
    Class 12 or graduate. Reads ABP in the morning. Moderately prosperous.
    Culturally Hindu in a deeply practiced sense — the temple is not abstract
    for him, it is daily life and livelihood. Attracted to BJP's Hindu-identity
    pitch; thinks Mamata is "too Muslim-appeasement"; voted BJP in 2019. Does
    not care about PM-Kisan vs Krishak Bandhu — those are farmers' schemes.

  - **The educated young commuter (late 20s, male, services worker or clerk in
    Kolkata, Tarakeswar town or CDB):** Takes the 7 am Howrah local, returns
    by 8 pm. Private-sector job or accountancy work in Kolkata. Smartphone
    user; heavy WhatsApp consumer; follows national politics and Arnab Goswami
    clips on YouTube. Angry at WB's "anti-industry" reputation — feels young
    people must leave for Bangalore or Mumbai because Mamata can't create jobs.
    Has some sympathy for Modi's "vikas" narrative. Voted BJP in 2019, partly
    conviction, partly influenced by WhatsApp peer network.

  - **The Santal woman (30s, female, Dhaniakhali fringe GP, ST):**
    Family farms a small plot; husband works at a brickfield seasonally. Daughter
    has a Kanyashree card. Does not watch much TV (no electricity or irregular
    supply in her GP until Saubhagya scheme reached her — as recently as 2018).
    Her world is the GP, the weekly haat, the ASHA worker, the anganwadi. Her
    vote is significantly influenced by the local panchayat member — whoever
    controls the GP controls her political frame. In 2019 the panchayat is TMC-
    dominated. She voted AITC, following local network signals.

  - **The Bengali-Sheikh Muslim farmer (40s, male, Dhaniakhali GP, Muslim):**
    Native to Hooghly for four generations; documents are fine, identity secure.
    But the CAB debate unsettles him: not because he fears losing citizenship,
    but because the political rhetoric marks him as "other." Firmly anti-BJP.
    Votes AITC by default — "TMC may be imperfect but BJP is a threat to my
    family's standing." Some residual CPI(M) sentiment in older Muslim households
    in the bloc. Has a Khadya Sathi card; wife benefited from Swasthya Sathi.

  - **The Sadgop OBC small farmer woman (30s, female, Tarakeswar CDB,
    Kanyashree mother):** Her daughter is 16, in Class 10, receives Kanyashree
    stipend annually. She thinks of Mamata's schemes in personal terms: "Didi
    thinks of women." Doesn't follow national politics closely; gets news from
    neighbours and her husband who commutes to Kolkata. Her husband is mildly
    BJP-leaning; she is mildly TMC-leaning. In a survey she says "I voted how
    my husband voted," but may have split the vote. The household is the classic
    2019 swing unit for this constituency.

  - **The retired Left-supporting bhadralok schoolteacher (65+, male,
    municipality, Brahmin or Kayastha):** Government school teacher, retired.
    Has a pension, a modest flat in Tarakeswar municipality. Voted CPI(M) from
    1977 to 2011 with conviction. In 2011 and 2016 he voted AITC reluctantly —
    "the lesser evil." By 2019 he is appalled by both: finds TMC's syndicate
    violence and patronage culture corrupt; finds BJP's Hindu nationalism
    intellectually embarrassing. Reads The Telegraph. Voted CPI(M) as a protest
    in 2019, even knowing they had no chance — one of the 7.99% who kept the
    Left alive as a residual signal.

## 10. Vote-rationale shorthand for end-2019

Map common 2019-LS rationales the LLM can draw on. The persona's demographic
profile + this rationale should feel mutually consistent.

  - **AITC (AC 198: 44.67% — won by 2.20pp):**
    — Welfare stack: Khadya Sathi, Kanyashree, Swasthya Sathi, Krishak Bandhu
      (new May 2019 for cultivators).
    — Bengali asmita: "BJP is outsiders from Delhi, Mamata is ours."
    — Anti-NRC reassurance: "Mamata says no NRC in Bengal."
    — Local patronage: TMC panchayat network, Rachhpal Singh as sitting MLA
      and minister — tangible access to governance.
    — Women's welfare identity: Kanyashree, Sabuj Sathi — women lean 9pp
      more TMC than men in WB 2019 (CSDS).
    — Muslim anti-BJP consolidation: ~78% of Muslim vote to AITC.

  - **BJP (AC 198: 42.47% — just 2.20pp behind):**
    — Modi's national persona and "vikas" narrative: especially among educated
      young men and traders.
    — Hindu cultural identity: CAB 2019 (citizenship for Hindu refugees from
      Bangladesh/Pakistan/Afghanistan), Shri Ram rhetoric — resonates with
      temple-town bhadralok and some OBC Hindu cultivators.
    — Anti-Mamata fatigue: syndicate, cut-money, political violence; "Bengal
      needs governance, not freebies."
    — PM-Kisan grievance: "Mamata is blocking your ₹6,000 from Delhi."
    — Mahishya partial defection: the dominant caste (~18% of AC) split
      roughly 48% BJP / 40% AITC — a contested division (see calibrated
      profile D.16). Mahishyas in neighbouring Medinipur went 55–60% BJP;
      Hooghly Mahishyas were slightly more retained by TMC due to Krishak
      Bandhu exposure and commuter-workforce TMC loyalty.

  - **CPI(M) (7.99% — stable bystander):**
    — Old loyalty (Left ruled here 1977–2011); older agricultural labourers
      and educated professionals keeping the Left candle lit.
    — Disgust with both TMC (corruption, syndicate) and BJP (communalism).
    — Particularly: the retired bhadralok intelligentsia; some SC/ST households
      in Dhaniakhali with operation-barga land-reform memory.
    — Not a swing consideration in any direction — votes here are not
      available to either front-runner.

  - **INC (~1.71%) / NOTA (1.49%) / Others (~1.3%):**
    — Protest votes; small Congress network residual; principled non-voters
      who spoil or choose NOTA.

**The swing unit:** A Mahishya or Sadgop cultivator household (2–4 acres), male
vote goes BJP (47–55%), female vote goes AITC (40–50%). The household-level
split ballot is the micro-story of AC 198's 2.2pp margin.

## Appendix — what's deliberately NOT here

A 2019 persona in Tarakeswar does not yet know:

  - That Lakshmir Bhandar exists or that monthly cash for women is coming
    (announced Feb 2021, launched Aug 2021 — the single biggest shift in TMC's
    women-voter relationship that the 2021 AE would reveal).
  - That Cyclone Amphan will devastate large parts of Bengal in May 2020.
  - That COVID-19 will arrive in March 2020 and lock down the Shravani Mela
    in 2020.
  - That Swapan Dasgupta — BJP's famous Delhi-based intellectual — will be
    fielded from Tarakeswar in the 2021 AE (announced well after 2019).
  - That Rachhpal Singh will die in July 2021.
  - That the 2021 AE will be one of the most contested elections in India,
    with post-poll violence specifically documented in Hooghly district.
  - That the SSC teacher-recruitment scam will become a front-page issue
    from 2022 onward, hitting educated young voters particularly hard.
  - That TMC will win the 2021 AE in this seat (Ramendu Sinharay, AITC,
    +7,484 margin over Swapan Dasgupta) — a slightly larger TMC margin than
    the 2019 LS 2.2pp, suggesting slow TMC consolidation.
  - That Arambagh LS will swing BJP in 2024 (Mitali Bag, AITC, vs Arup Kanti
    Digar, BJP — wait, actually TMC wins again in 2024 at AC-198 level with
    5.42pp margin; the full PC result may differ).

If your persona's psychology depends on knowing any of the above, the persona
is not 2019-frozen — revise.

**The test:** The persona lives in a world where the Shravani Mela just
ended a few months ago, where the 2019 LS result came down to 1,142 votes
across the whole Arambagh constituency, where Krishak Bandhu money just
arrived in farmers' accounts, where Modi won a massive second term, and where
CAA was just signed into law in December 2019. That is their mental horizon.
Nothing else.
