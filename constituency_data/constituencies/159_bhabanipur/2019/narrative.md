# Bhabanipur (AC 159) — pre-2019 / end-2019 opinion context

**Purpose.** Narrative anchors for the LLM persona generator (Phase 1.2). Each
persona's psychology, salience topics, and 2019 vote rationale should draw on
the conditions described here. This is **the world the persona lived in
through end-2019** — nothing later.

> ⛔ **Strict 2019-frozen scope.** Do NOT reference: COVID-19 / Cyclone Amphan
> (2020), Lakshmir Bhandar (Aug 2021), Mamata's Nandigram defeat and
> Bhabanipur by-election (Sep 2021), SSC scam exposure (2022+), SIR voter-roll
> revision (2025–26), or any news after 31 December 2019. The CAB → CAA
> passage (Dec 11, 2019) IS in scope as an end-2019 live event; its
> implementation rules and aftermath are not. Chandra Kumar Bose's later
> departure from BJP (2023) and switch to TMC (2026) are NOT in scope.

## 1. Who lives here

**AC 159 Bhabanipur** sits in South-Central Kolkata, spanning eight KMC wards
(Nos. 63, 70, 71, 72, 73, 74, 77, 82) of Borough VIII. It is 100% urban — no
gram panchayat, no agricultural land, no MGNREGA relevance. The constituency
is bounded by Gariahat to the east, Kalighat to the south-west, and the
Kidderpur/Garden Reach fringe to the west. The Kalighat temple, some of South
Kolkata's best English-medium schools (La Martiniere, St. Xavier's, Ashutosh
College), and the Rashbehari Avenue commercial spine are all in or adjacent to
this AC.

The electorate (200,938 in 2019) divides into three roughly equal blocs —
making Bhabanipur the "mini-India" of WB's assembly map:

- **Bengali Hindu (~42%)** — bhadralok (Brahmin/Kayastha/Baidya upper-castes
  ~25%, Bengali OBC/middle-caste ~15%, Bengali SC ~2%) concentrated in the
  northern ward cluster (Wards 63, 70, 71, 72 — the Bhowanipore core and Hazra
  Road belt). Multigenerational residents of old Kolkata families; professional
  class (teachers, govt officers, doctors, lawyers), retired civil servants,
  and college students. This is the bhadralok heartland of South Kolkata.

- **Non-Bengali Hindu (~34%)** — dominated by Marwari business families
  (~10.4% of electorate; The Print/IANS), Gujarati Hindu traders (~8%,
  Lansdowne–Chakraberia–Pudda Pukur belt), Bihari/UP labour and trade migrants
  (~7%), Punjabi-Sikh households (~1.5%, with a gurudwara presence on
  Bhowanipore corridor), and Jain business families (~0.8%, Elgin Road area).
  Concentrated in Wards 70, 71, 72, 73, 74. These communities have deep
  business roots — many families arrived 2–3 generations ago for Kolkata's
  commercial trade. This non-Bengali bloc is structurally the most BJP-oriented
  segment in the AC.

- **Muslim (~24%)** — higher than the KMC district average (Census 2011 Kolkata
  20.60%). Concentrated in the south-west ward cluster, especially Ward 77
  (Patuapara/Kidderpur fringe, the largest ward by population at ~45,984
  persons with 83.84% literacy) and Ward 82 (Kidderpur-adjacent). Sub-groups:
  Bengali Sheikh (old-resident Kolkata Muslim ~45%), Bihari/UP Muslim migrant
  (~25%, Hindi/Urdu-speaking), Urdu-dominant artisan-Muslim (~15%), Pathan/
  Sayyid fringe (~8%). Nearly monolithic TMC voters in 2019.

**Economic character:** Upper-middle income. ~35–45% of households are
trader/self-employed (Marwari/Gujarati/Jain commerce, small retail on
Rashbehari Avenue and Gariahat corridor). ~30–40% are formal-salaried
(bhadralok government and private-sector employees). BPL households are a
small minority (~5–8%) concentrated in Ward 77's labour pockets. The AC has no
agricultural or MGNREGA footprint. Four-wheeler ownership (~20% of HHs),
smartphone penetration (~78%), and graduate+ share (~28–35%) are the highest
among WB's non-metro ACs and comparable to South Mumbai or South Delhi in
media sophistication.

**Educational and cultural register:** Bhabanipur's voters are far more
English-media-consuming than any rural or semi-urban AC in the simulation.
English newspapers (The Telegraph, Times of India Kolkata edition) are read
alongside Anandabazar Patrika. Senior bhadralok households have cable TV
(ABP Ananda + Zee 24 Ghanta are primary) and many also follow Republic TV or
NDTV. Marwari/Gujarati trader households often follow Hindi business channels
(CNBC Awaaz, Zee Business) in addition to Bengali news. Smartphone penetration
is near-saturation among voters under 50, with active WhatsApp group usage in
every community bloc.

**The BJP candidate, Chandra Kumar Bose**, is the grand-nephew of Netaji
Subhas Chandra Bose. This was his second Bhabanipur-linked campaign — he had
contested the 2016 Assembly election from Bhabanipur itself as a BJP candidate
(losing to Mamata Banerjee). His Netaji lineage was explicitly invoked in
BJP's campaign. For the bhadralok voter who reveres Netaji as a Bengali icon
second only to Tagore, Chandra Kumar Bose carried a symbolic appeal that went
beyond ordinary BJP candidacy — the subtext being "even Netaji's family
supports BJP over TMC." BJP's campaign paired this local Netaji resonance with
the national Modi wave. Chandra Kumar Bose had earlier worked to transfer
Netaji's cap to PM Modi for the Netaji Museum at Red Fort (January 2019) — an
act that received coverage in Bengali media and helped BJP claim ownership of
the Netaji legacy nationally. For CAA-skeptical Bengali bhadralok, the Netaji
framing provided a culturally comfortable BJP entry point that didn't require
fully endorsing Hindutva.

State-wide context: BJP went from 2 → 18 LS seats in Bengal; TMC held 22
(down from 34). CPI(M) went to zero LS seats. The narrative that Bengal was
heading toward a real two-party (TMC vs BJP) contest entered the bloodstream
this year.

## 3. The bhadralok BJP swing — what drove it

The 2019 bhadralok swing toward BJP in urban Kolkata is the most consequential
new political fact for Bhabanipur as of end-2019. Understanding it requires
distinguishing the bhadralok's layered grievances from simple Hindu nationalism.

**Anti-incumbency on law and order.** The TMC government, in power since 2011,
had accumulated a thick record of political violence in West Bengal. The 2018
panchayat election was particularly damaging to TMC's urban image — large-scale
"rigging by omission" (opposition candidates prevented from filing
nominations), followed by post-poll violence. For the educated, English-media-
consuming bhadralok in South Kolkata drawing rooms, these events — reported
extensively in The Telegraph, and amplified by national media — raised a
governance question: "Is this how we run elections in Bengal?" The 2019 LS
campaign itself saw the Vidyasagar College statue controversy (the bust of
Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar, revered as the founder of modern Bengali education,
was damaged during a BJP rally in Amherst Street on 14 May 2019) — widely
attributed by TMC to BJP, widely disputed by BJP. The incident inflamed Bengali
bhadralok sentiment and was a last-week campaign controversy. Both parties
tried to weaponize it; neither fully succeeded in Bhabanipur.

**The "appeasement" narrative.** BJP's central charge in urban Kolkata was that
Mamata Banerjee's TMC practised minority appeasement — using state resources
and administrative patronage to favour Muslim-majority areas, providing
imams a state stipend (the imamate stipend was a real, widely covered issue),
and allowing anti-Hindu communal violence in rural WB to go unpunished. For
the bhadralok Hindu voter who had always thought of himself as secular and
anti-BJP, the calculation by 2019 was more complicated: "I don't like Hindutva
but Mamata's model is also becoming communal in its own way — it just
appease the other side."

**CSDS data on Hindu swing.** CSDS-Lokniti 2019 NES shows that BJP's vote
share among Hindus in West Bengal went from 21% (2014) to 57% (2019). TMC's
vote among Hindus fell from 40% to 32%. This was not uniform across the state
— the deepest swing was in tribal belts and North Bengal — but urban Kolkata
bhadralok participation in the Hindu consolidation was real and visible in the
Bhabanipur margin (BJP at 42.60% vs 44.92% AITC).

**The Modi persona.** Among urban, economically active voters — including
Marwari/Gujarati business families and even some Bengali middle-class
professionals — Narendra Modi's 2014–2019 record held appeal: GST (disruptive
but seen as a national modernisation), PMJDY banking, the optics of a
"decisive" central government contrasted with what the bhadralok saw as
TMC's patronage-driven chaos. The national Modi-wave was felt in Bhabanipur's
commercial wards.

## 4. The Gujarati-Marwari business community and BJP loyalty

Bhabanipur's ~18–20% non-Bengali business community (Marwari ~10.4% + Gujarati
~8%) is the single most BJP-loyal demographic bloc in the constituency. This
loyalty is structural, not conjunctural, and predates the 2019 Modi wave.

**Historical alignment.** Marwari and Gujarati business families in Kolkata
have been attached to the national Hindu right since the Jana Sangh era. The
RSS has maintained consistent organisational presence among Marwari trading
families in Kolkata since the 1950s. These are communities whose cultural
identity (Vaishnavite Marwari Hinduism, Jain business ethics) aligns naturally
with BJP's urban Hindu nationalism. In 2019, with Narendra Modi — himself a
Gujarati — as PM, the Gujarati community's BJP vote was near-monolithic.

**Business grievances against TMC.** The Marwari/Gujarati trader community had
specific anti-TMC grievances by 2019: (a) party-level extortion ("syndicate"
culture, where local TMC operators demanded cuts from construction, logistics,
and small-business activity); (b) erratic law enforcement, which they
attributed to TMC's patronage-based policing; (c) a preference for the
national-level stability of a Modi government over a Bengal-first Chief
Minister. The GST (rolled out 1 July 2017) caused short-term chaos for small
traders — and Marwari/Gujarati traders were disproportionately exposed — but
by the 2019 election many had largely absorbed the transition and re-oriented
toward national BJP as a "national party that governs with rules."

**Vote estimate:** D.16 in the calibrated profile puts Marwari at 68% BJP,
Gujarati at 70% BJP for the 2019 LS. The business community's contribution to
BJP's 57,969-vote total in Bhabanipur is load-bearing — without them, BJP's
number falls significantly.

**The TMC counter.** TMC is not absent from this bloc. The party has cultivated
relationships with prominent Marwari and Gujarati businessmen — especially
those with large real-estate interests in Kolkata, who need state government
cooperation for permits and land. A subset of affluent, pragmatic business
families voted TMC or abstained in 2019 as a business hedge. TMC's narrative:
"You can run your business in Bengal with us; BJP will bring Delhi interference
and IB scrutiny."

## 5. CAA/NRC in an urban, educated electorate

CAA/NRC plays out very differently in Bhabanipur than in rural WB or even in
the Matua belt. The urban educated electorate here processes citizenship
politics through a rights-and-governance frame rather than a personal-papers
anxiety frame.

**Timeline as of end-2019:**
- **Assam NRC final list published: 31 August 2019.** 1.9 million excluded;
  significantly includes Bengali Hindus alongside Bengali Muslims. Mamata
  Banerjee's "no NRC in Bengal" stance consolidated. For Bhabanipur's bhadralok,
  the Assam NRC reading was: "This is badly administered and can hit Bengalis
  of all religions — including us." Unlike Bangaon's refugee-origin Matua
  community (who lived the documentation anxiety personally), Bhabanipur
  bhadralok reacted with *political* concern rather than *existential* fear.
- **CAB introduced in 17th Lok Sabha: November 2019.** Passed in Lok Sabha 9
  December 2019. Passed in Rajya Sabha 11 December 2019. Signed into law 12
  December 2019. **As of end-2019, implementation rules had not been notified.**
- **Anti-CAA protests:** Large protests began immediately. In Kolkata, Mamata
  Banerjee led a major rally on 26 December 2019 from Raja Bazar to Mullick
  Bazar. Park Circus Maidan saw a sit-in protest. Bhabanipur's Ward 77 Muslim
  residents participated; several bhadralok civil society figures also joined.

**How different communities in Bhabanipur read it:**

*Bhadralok (Bengali Hindu upper-caste):* Split and uncomfortable. Most
bhadralok are secular in self-image — they identify with Tagore's syncretic
Bengal, not the RSS's Hindu Rashtra. CAA felt to many like "we don't need this
divisive thing." But a countercurrent existed: some bhadralok — especially those
with family memory of Partition displacement from East Bengal — quietly endorsed
the principle of protecting non-Muslim refugees. The dominant bhadralok
editorial position (The Telegraph editorial board, college professors,
Presidency alumni networks) was anti-CAA. The Vidyasagar statue controversy
and the Modi government's perceived disregard for Bengali cultural identity
deepened bhadralok ambivalence about BJP even as many voted for it in May 2019.

*Marwari/Gujarati community:* Largely supportive of CAA, mildly. These
communities don't carry the Partition refugee anxiety but do appreciate
BJP's Hindu-identity signal. Some Jain voices noted the CAA explicitly
protects Jains from Bangladesh/Pakistan as a class. Mostly quiet on NRC,
which they see as applying to poor-documentation populations, not them.

*Muslim community (24% of AC):* Uniformly hostile. The CAA's exclusion of
Muslims from the citizenship pathway was experienced as a direct stigmatization
— "the government is formally saying Muslims are second-class potential
citizens." Ward 77's residents followed the protests closely; anti-CAA WhatsApp
messaging was intense in December 2019. Voting behavior: near-total TMC
consolidation, already high in 2019 (estimated 75% TMC per D.15), would
harden further after CAA passed.

*Urban Muslim service class (in-migrant, Bihari/UP-origin in Ward 82):* More
acutely anxious than established Bengali Muslims — these families have weaker
documentation and know that NRC, if it came to WB, would expose them first.
Mamata's "no NRC" was their political lifeline.

**The Mamata anti-CAA pivot.** Mamata was the first mainstream CM to oppose
CAA/NRC publicly and consistently. For Bhabanipur's Muslim voters and for
some bhadralok anti-BJP voters, this was the single strongest reason to not
defect to BJP's side in 2019. Her December 2019 street rallies in Kolkata
were the clearest sign that TMC was planting its flag firmly on the anti-CAA
ground — a political position that played well in the urban educated + Muslim
coalition that Bhabanipur's demographics required.

## 6. The TMC welfare stack — less relevant here, but present

Bhabanipur is the AC in the simulation where the TMC welfare stack has the
weakest bite. The constituency has no agricultural population, so Krishak
Bandhu is irrelevant. Lakshmir Bhandar does not exist in 2019. But several
schemes do reach voters here:

- **Khadya Sathi** (subsidized PDS rice, Jan 2016): ~65–75% household coverage
  even in this affluent AC, because the scheme covers APL households at modest
  cost. Not a strong vote driver for upper-middle-income bhadralok or Marwari/
  Gujarati families, who use private markets. High relevance for Ward 77's
  working-class Muslim households.
- **Swasthya Sathi** (health insurance up to Rs 5 lakh, Dec 2016): ~70–80%
  family coverage estimated. Modest TMC-tilt in urban Bhabanipur vs the strong
  tilt in rural ACs — because this electorate has greater access to private
  insurance and private hospitals. The scheme helps most for lower-income Muslim
  and informal-worker households.
- **Kanyashree Prakalpa** (girl-child education stipend, launched 2013): ~3–5k
  active beneficiaries in this AC — lower than rural ACs because the private-
  school-heavy electorate often doesn't qualify. Still present among Bengali
  OBC/SC and Muslim households.
- **Sabuj Sathi** (bicycles for Class 9–12 students): Low coverage in urban KMC
  — private-school students generally don't receive.
- **Duare Sarkar** (government services at the doorstep): Not launched until
  2020 — OUT OF SCOPE in 2019.

**The net welfare calculation for Bhabanipur's TMC vote:** Unlike rural ACs
where the welfare stack is the dominant TMC retention mechanism, in Bhabanipur
the welfare stack is a floor, not a driver. TMC's urban support rests more on
Mamata's personal identity, anti-BJP consolidation among Muslims, and the
community-club political machine than on beneficiary loyalty. For affluent non-
Bengali traders who use none of the schemes, the welfare argument is
irrelevant; for Ward 77's working-class residents, Khadya Sathi and Swasthya
Sathi are real.

## 7. Mamata Banerjee's personal political identity — Bhabanipur as her turf

Bhabanipur is not merely a constituency Mamata Banerjee contests from — it is
the site of her political formation and her personal home. She grew up here,
studied at Jogamaya Devi College (Bhowanipore area), worked a milk-booth job
in her youth in these lanes, and her Kalighat residence is within the
constituency boundary. As The Print notes: "Its lanes and community clubs were
central to her rise from Kolkata South MP to Bengal's most dominant politician."

**The "ghorer meye" (daughter of the house) identity.** In a constituency
where Mamata has not formally been on the Assembly ballot since 2011 (she
contested as MP before creating the CM-seat arrangement), the relationship is
quasi-personal. Voters in Bhowanipore have known her since before she became
Chief Minister — they remember her at local Durga Puja pandals, her
no-security style of walking through market lanes, her visible anger at the
Left government in her street-fighter phase. This "didi is one of us" identity
is a genuine emotional asset with the Bengali Hindu electorate that transcends
ordinary incumbency.

**The 2011 arrangement.** When Mamata became CM in May 2011, she engineered a
two-step: her loyal aide Subrata Bakshi won Bhabanipur in April 2011 by
~50,000 votes, then vacated the seat in October 2011 so Mamata could contest
the by-election and enter the Assembly as CM-elect. Mamata won the October 2011
by-poll with ~77% share, a blowout that reflected the entire anti-Left wave
channeled into one seat. Then in 2016, she contested Bhabanipur again (BJP was
not the main challenger — INC's Deepa Dasmunshi came second) and won with
65,520 votes (47.7%) against Dasmunshi's 40,219 (29.3%). BJP's role in 2016
Bhabanipur was minor.

**The shift by 2019.** By 2019, Mamata no longer contests this seat at
Assembly level (she does not have an AE here in 2019). But her name is on
Kolkata Dakshin LS territory — Mala Roy is the candidate, but Mamata's personal
relationship with Bhabanipur's voters is the campaign's emotional core. Every
TMC booth-committee meeting in these eight wards invokes Didi. The question
"would Bhabanipur embarrass Mamata?" is the subtext of the 3,168-vote margin
result. It nearly did: BJP's 42.60% in Bhabanipur was the second-highest BJP
share in any AC within Kolkata Dakshin LS in 2019, driven by the Marwari/
Gujarati bloc consolidating and the bhadralok swing.

**What voters feel about Mamata personally, end-2019:**
- *Bengali Hindu bhadralok (older, TMC-leaning):* "She's our own — rough
  around the edges, not intellectual, but she cares about this neighbourhood.
  She built our puja pandal stage, got the road fixed. I don't like the
  syndicate boys but Didi herself is accessible."
- *Bengali Hindu bhadralok (younger, BJP-drifting):* "Mamata used to be a
  reformer. Now it's all muscle and minority politics. The culture of the
  state has changed — she rewards loyalty, not merit."
- *Gujarati/Marwari trader:* "We respect her for keeping Kolkata stable — no
  communal riots. But she doesn't understand business. The syndicate costs us
  money. Modi governs, she campaigns."
- *Muslim voter (Ward 77):* "Didi is the only wall between us and BJP. I
  don't ask for more."

## 8. Media diet (urban Kolkata — richer media environment)

Bhabanipur's media environment is qualitatively different from any rural or
semi-urban AC in the simulation. This affects how narratives form and how
quickly political information penetrates.

**Bengali print (high reach):**
- *Anandabazar Patrika* — dominant Bengali daily, read in virtually every
  Bengali Hindu household; broadly centrist editorially but carries extensive
  political coverage.
- *Ei Samay* — TMC-sympathetic Bengali daily; strong in bhadralok households.
- *Bartaman* — more neutral; popular among Bengali traders and middle-class.
- *Sangbad Pratidin* — TMC-leaning.

**English print (unusually high for a WB AC):**
- *The Telegraph (Kolkata edition)* — the intellectual bhadralok's newspaper;
  runs sharp political editorials; was vocally critical of both BJP's Vidyasagar-
  statue incident and TMC's political violence record in 2019. Graduate+
  households (28–35% of AC) often have a Telegraph subscription.
- *Times of India (Kolkata edition)* — more conservative-national; read by
  business community.
- *Hindustan Times Kolkata* — smaller reach.

**Bengali TV (dominant for 55+ cohort):**
- *ABP Ananda* — most-watched Bengali news channel; relatively balanced.
- *Zee 24 Ghanta* — second-most-watched; broader popular appeal.
- *News18 Bangla* — BJP-sympathetic signal perceived by viewers; popular in
  BJP-leaning households.

**Hindi TV (Marwari/Gujarati/Bihari households, ~28% of electorate):**
- *Aaj Tak, Zee News, Republic TV (Hindi)* — strong pro-BJP signal. These
  channels reach the non-Bengali Hindu bloc and amplify national BJP narratives
  that might not penetrate Bengali-language media.
- *CNBC Awaaz / Zee Business* — business news; high reach in trader HHs.

**Digital / WhatsApp:**
- Smartphone penetration ~78% in this AC by 2019 (highest in WB simulation
  set). WhatsApp is the dominant political rumour/forwarding medium.
- Each community bloc has active WhatsApp groups: bhadralok alumni networks
  (Presidency, La Martiniere, Ashutosh), Marwari trader chamber groups,
  Gujarati Samaj groups, Muslim ward-level groups.
- BJP runs an organized WhatsApp forward network (BJP IT cell is active in
  Kolkata urban); TMC has party WhatsApp trees via booth committees.

**Media diet interaction with political opinion:**
The multilingual, multi-channel media environment means Bhabanipur voters
receive genuinely competing narratives — not a single TMC-dominant frame.
A bhadralok voter consuming The Telegraph + ABP Ananda + some NDTV gets a
fairly balanced picture; a Marwari trader consuming Aaj Tak + CNBC Awaaz +
BJP WhatsApp gets a strongly BJP-national frame. This divergence in media
diet between the Bengali bloc and the non-Bengali trader bloc directly tracks
the vote split the calibrated profile shows.

## 9. Common psychological archetypes

Use these as starting templates — vary, blend, and complicate them with the

## 10. Vote-rationale shorthand for end-2019

Map common 2019-LS rationales the LLM can draw on. The persona's
demographic profile + this rationale should feel mutually consistent.

- **BJP (42.60% in AC 159, 35.07% LS-wide)** — the Marwari/Gujarati business
  community near-monolith (68–70% BJP); bhadralok anti-TMC anti-incumbency
  swing (~43–45% of Bengali Hindu bhadralok voted BJP, per CSDS WB urban);
  Modi national wave; Chandra Kumar Bose's Netaji-lineage resonance with
  culturally Hindu Bengali voters; anti-Muslim-appeasement framing; Hindu
  consolidation (CSDS: 57% of WB Hindus → BJP in 2019 vs 21% in 2014); and
  anti-CAA anxiety driving some voters *away* from BJP but not enough to hold
  the March-May pre-CAA wave.

- **AITC (44.92% in AC 159, 48.11% LS-wide)** — near-total Muslim vote
  consolidation (est. 75% of 24% Muslim share = ~18pp contribution); Mamata's
  personal Bhabanipur identity and the "ghorer meye" emotional bond; welfare
  floor for Ward 77's working class; Bengali-middle-caste and Bengali OBC
  loyalty (38–50% TMC per caste groups); anti-BJP consolidation among secular
  bhadralok; Mamata's "no NRC" as a credible anti-CAA signal; community-club
  machine strength in Bhowanipore.

- **CPI(M) (6.39% in AC 159)** — residual bhadralok Left loyalty among 60+
  voters with CPI(M) history; protest votes from young bhadralok who are
  anti-BJP but also anti-TMC-corruption; candidate Nandini Mukherjee is a
  credible local Left face. The Left's collapse from 28% in 2011 AE to 6.39%
  in 2019 LS represents the near-total erosion of the old Bengali bhadralok
  Left bloc — most of it moved to TMC in 2011, and some of that TMC share has
  now moved to BJP.

- **INC (3.43% in AC 159)** — small residual INC voter (older South Kolkata
  Congress families; some Bengali professionals with INC nostalgia); candidate
  Mita Chakraborty has low name recognition. The Congress is a non-factor in
  this AC by 2019.

- **Others + NOTA (2.66%)** — educated protest voters, NOTA from bhadralok
  disillusioned with all parties.

**The 2019 Bhabanipur coalition in one sentence:** Mamata held her seat with
a razor-thin 3,168-vote margin by retaining Muslim voters almost entirely,
keeping enough Bengali bhadralok from defecting, and benefiting from a
split between the bhadralok BJPswingers (42%) and the welfare-anchor / Didi-
loyalty AITC base (45%). The Marwari/Gujarati bloc voted BJP ~70%, but at ~18%
of the electorate they could not flip the seat alone.

persona is not 2019-frozen — revise.
