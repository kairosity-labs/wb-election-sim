## 1. Who lives here

**AC 11 Kalchini** sits in the eastern Dooars, at the foot of the Bhutan
border. The constituency = all 11 GPs of Kalchini CD Block (Chuapara,
Dalsingpara, Garopara, Jaigaon I, Jaigaon II, Kalchini, Latabari, Malangi,
Mendabari, Rajabhatkhawa, Satali) plus Majherdabri GP from Alipurduar II CD
Block. It is ST-reserved — one of only a handful of reserved ACs in North
Bengal — reflecting that roughly 40% of the population holds Scheduled Tribe
status.

The dominant human landscape is the **tea garden (bagan)**: rows of plucking
bushes stretching to the foothills, housing lines of brick and tin where
workers live in company-provided quarters, and the steepled chimney of the
factory rising above it. The constituency contains approximately 18 tea
gardens within the Kalchini CD Block alone, and the signature of the
workforce — Oraon (Kurukh-speaking), Munda, Santhal, Kharia, Gond — is the
Chhotanagpur Adivasi diaspora that British planters relocated to Dooars as
indentured labour in the 19th century. Fourth and fifth generations now born
here call themselves native sons and daughters of the Dooars, yet maintain
Jharkhand-origin tribal culture, languages, and the Sadri lingua franca.

Jaigaon, the largest census town (pop. ~42,000 in 2011), stands directly on
the India–Bhutan border; the Bhutan Gate separates it from Phuentsholing.
The town has its own economy — border traders, logistics, the Bengali
managerial and shop-owning class — distinct from the bagan world a few
kilometres into the interior. A Nepali/Gorkha community (~26% of Kalchini CDB
mother-tongue speakers are Nepali) intersects both worlds: some are bagan
supervisors, some are independent cultivators or traders in the forested fringes.
A small Boro and Rabha tribal population inhabits the forest-edge villages.
Muslims (~8% of the CDB) are concentrated in peripheral GPs and are largely
Bengali-speaking.

Electorate in 2019: **235,753** (ECI source). Turnout in 2019 LS: **80.8%**
(190,453 valid votes). Literacy ~69% (2011 CDB base); female literacy ~60%.
About 45% of households are estimated to be BPL at the tea-garden wage of
₹176/day. Smartphone internet penetration is ~28% — one of the lowest among
WB ACs — and media consumption is dominated by Sadri-language oral networks,
Hindi-language radio, and Nepali TV for the Gorkha segment.

## 2. The 2019 LS political shock

In the 2019 Lok Sabha election, the **Alipurduars (ST)** LS seat — which
covers Kalchini AC as one of seven assembly segments — recorded one of the
most decisive victories in North Bengal:

- **John Barla (BJP)**: 750,804 votes / **54.36%**, won
- **Dasrath Tirkey (AITC)**: 506,815 votes / **36.69%**, lost by 243,989
- **Mili Oraon (RSP)**: 54,010 / **3.91%** — Left Front collapse complete
- BJP margin over AITC at the LS level: **17.67 pp**

At the **AC 11 Kalchini segment specifically**, the BJP's margin was even
wider: John Barla took **58.53%** (111,477 votes) against Dasrath Tirkey's
**33.63%** (64,045 votes) — a **24.90 pp** lead on 190,453 valid votes.
Kalchini was the BJP's second-strongest segment in the Alipurduars LS
constellation, confirming it as a BTWU-organized stronghold.

John Barla is not an outsider imported by the BJP. He is an Oraon Adivasi
from Jalpaiguri district who worked as a tea-garden labourer from age 14,
rose through the Akhil Bharatiya Adivasi Vikas Parishad (ABAVP), became
president of the **Bharatiya Tea Workers' Union (BTWU)** — the RSS-affiliated
union active in 199 of 408 tea gardens in the Darjeeling/Alipurduar/Jalpaiguri
belt — and joined the BJP in 2014. His win in 2019 is simultaneously a
**class victory** (organised labour shifting political alignment) and an
**identity victory** (an Adivasi worker defeating both the Bengali TMC
candidate and the Left's long-standing Adivasi MP tradition). For a Kalchini
bagan worker, John Barla is "one of us who made it."

The Left Front's collapse at this seat is historically significant. For three
decades (1977–2009), Kalchini and the broader Alipurduars belt were RSP
territory: Manohar Tirkey, an Adivasi RSP leader, held the Alipurduars LS
seat from 1977 until 2009, and RSP won the Kalchini AE seat repeatedly. The
Adivasi vote was the Left's foundational constituency in the Dooars. By 2019
the RSP was down to 3.91% LS-wide and 2.20% at AC 11. The 35-year Left–Adivasi
bond, already fractured by the GJM-backed IND interlude in 2009–2011 and the
razor-thin AITC win in 2016 AE, finally snapped — and the BJP, not TMC,
received the transferred loyalty.

State-wide context: BJP went from 2 → 18 LS seats in Bengal in 2019; TMC
held 22 (down from 34). In North Bengal (8 LS seats), BJP won 7 — a near-
sweep that would have been unthinkable in 2014. The narrative that Bengal was
moving toward a genuine two-party BJP-vs-TMC contest entered the air. In the
bagan lines of Kalchini, this was felt not as an abstract national number but
as a personal outcome: "our man won."

## 3. The tea-garden Adivasi identity axis — the dominant story

The defining cleavage in Kalchini is not Hindu vs Muslim or Bengali vs non-
Bengali in the way it defines other WB ACs. It is the **bagan world vs
everyone else**, and within the bagan world, the question of who speaks for
the Adivasi worker.

**The bagan structure.** Tea gardens in the Dooars are privately owned
plantations (some by large corporates, some by smaller Indian owners, some by
absentee owners who have let gardens become "sick"). Workers live in company-
provided housing lines (tolas), receive a daily wage, and are nominally covered
under the Plantation Labour Act (1951), which obligates management to provide
housing, water, rations, medical care, and a school. In practice, enforcement
is deeply uneven. The garden management hierarchy — garden manager (usually
Bengali or South Indian), assistant manager, babu (clerk), sirdar (line
supervisor, often Adivasi) — runs parallel to the GP and block panchayat
structure. Within the bagan, the sirdar and the union representative have far
more daily authority over workers' lives than the panchayat pradhan.

**The BTWU organizing model.** The BTWU (BJP-aligned, RSS-supported) organized
steadily in the Dooars from the mid-2000s onward, filling the vacuum left by
the RSP and INTTUC (the AITC union) after the Left Front's decline. By 2019,
BTWU claimed presence in 199 of 408 North Bengal gardens. Their pitch was
twofold: (a) wage demand and arrears recovery — "the Left failed to get you
a living wage in 35 years; we will"; (b) Adivasi cultural identity —
"you are not just tea-garden workers, you are Adivasis, and we honour that."
John Barla as BTWU president embodied this synthesis. He and his union
canvassed garden lines physically in a way the TMC's Trinamul Cha Bagan
Sramik Union (TCBSU) and the now-collapsed RSP never matched in 2019.

**The RSP legacy and its end.** For the older generation (55+), the RSP was
the party that gave them dignity in a feudal plantation structure — the party
that got them permanency, ration, and the right to speak back to management.
Manohar Tirkey (RSP MP, 1977–2009) was an Adivasi of the tea belt; his
candidacy meant the community could see itself in Parliament. By 2019, the
RSP was a ghostly name. Mili Oraon (RSP candidate in 2019) got 4,194 votes
at AC 11 — 2.20%. The old vote didn't return to the Left; it went to the BJP,
which had copied the RSP's organizational strategy (union + cultural identity)
and added the Modi premium.

**The Christian Adivasi sub-community.** Mission churches — Catholic,
Protestant (Baptist, Lutheran) — have been active in the Dooars tea-belt since
the 19th century, converting significant shares of Oraon and Munda workers.
Christian Adivasis (~15% of Kalchini CDB, mostly Adivasi-origin) are better
educated on average (mission school effect), more literate, and slightly more
politically independent. They have some residual hesitation about the BJP's
Hindutva cultural framing but respond strongly to the BTWU's economic and
identity messaging. In 2019, even Christian Adivasi wards swung BJP —
evidence that the wage-arrear and identity-recognition narratives cut across
the Hindu/Christian divide within the Adivasi community.

**The ST reservation and seat identity.** Kalchini AC being ST-reserved means
only a Scheduled Tribe candidate can contest. This concentrates political
competition on which Adivasi leader — and which party backing them — can claim
authentic representation of the community. The BJP's advantage is Barla's
personal legitimacy. TMC's counterargument is welfare delivery.

## 4. NRC/CAA in the Dooars — a different anxiety from Matua country

The NRC–CAA issue plays out very differently in Kalchini than in the Matua
belt (Bangaon, Gaighata) or in Muslim-majority areas. The tea-garden Adivasi
community faces a **distinctive documentation vulnerability** rooted in
colonial history.

**The documentation problem.** Tea-garden workers were brought to the Dooars
as indentured migrants from Chhotanagpur from the 1860s through the 1930s.
Their descendants — 4th and 5th generation, born in the bagan, legally Indian
citizens — often have weak paper trails. The bagan "lines" are not
registered as residential addresses in the same way as village homesteads;
birth registrations in plantation hospitals were inconsistently maintained;
many workers lack any document older than a ration card or voter ID. If an
Assam-style NRC were extended to West Bengal, requiring a family tree of
documents back to a cut-off date, a substantial fraction of Kalchini's Adivasi
population could not comply — not because they are "foreigners" but because
the colonial plantation system never gave them proper paperwork.

**The 2019 NRC fallout — reading the Assam result.** When the final Assam NRC
was published on 31 August 2019, 1.9 million people were excluded. Among those
excluded were not just Bengali Muslims but approximately 500,000 Bengali Hindus
and a substantial number of Adivasi tea workers in Assam's tea belt (Assam's
Adivasi tea workers share the same documentation weakness as WB's). This
gave the Kalchini community a mirror to their own vulnerability: "if Assam's
bagan workers couldn't prove citizenship, what about us?" Mamata Banerjee's
"no NRC in Bengal" line registered as reassurance, but her party had just lost
the LS seat heavily — its credibility in the bagan was low.

**The CAA passage (Dec 2019) — muted comfort, not joy.** The CAA passed on
11–12 December 2019 offers citizenship protection to non-Muslim refugees from
Bangladesh, Pakistan, and Afghanistan. Kalchini's Adivasi workers are not
refugees — they are native citizens whose ancestors came from Jharkhand, not
Bangladesh. The CAA's citizenship path does not apply to them. For a Kalchini
Oraon or Munda household, the CAA is mostly irrelevant to their documentation
fear. The worry is not "will I be proven to be a Bangladeshi?" but "will
anyone ask me to prove I'm Indian and I won't have the papers?" The BJP's
answer to this community was not CAA but the political promise of tribal
recognition: "we are in power in Delhi, we will protect our own Adivasis."
That promise carried more weight in the bagan lines than the citizenship law
itself.

**Muslim minority in Kalchini.** The roughly 8% Muslim population (Bengali-
Muslim and some Bihari-origin Muslim in peripheral GPs) is intensely anti-
CAA. Their concern is the familiar one: the CAA excludes Muslims from the
citizenship fast-track, and if paired with an NRC, their status could be
challenged. Most Kalchini Muslims are firmly TMC voters (~65% TMC, ~15% INC in
the 2019 pattern). In a constituency where Adivasi ST voters dominate, Muslim
mobilization has limited seat-level impact but adds to the TMC vote floor.

## 5. The tea industry — wages, sick gardens, and structural precarity

**The daily wage as the central economic fact.** As of 2019, the
government-approved daily wage for tea-garden workers in West Bengal was
**₹176/day** (the 2015 wage revision; a further revision was pending and
contested). Against approximately 275 working days per year, this yields a
cash income of ~₹48,400/year (~₹4,000/month per working adult in the household).
This is below the state's own BPL calculation. The wage includes a ration
allowance component but excludes the cost of Provident Fund deductions.
In-kind benefits (housing, subsidized ration from the garden store, medical
dispensary) add nominal value but are unevenly maintained across gardens.

Unions (BTWU, INTTUC-affiliated TCBSU, and older CITU/RSP unions) had
been pressing for a wage revision for three years by 2019. BJP's pre-election
promise was that workers should receive at least ₹30,000 in accumulated
arrears from the three-year revision delay. This specific, tangible promise —
"money owed to you" — resonated more powerfully in the bagan lines than any
macro-economic speech.

**Sick and closed gardens.** The Dooars region has seen repeated garden
closures since the 1990s. Between 2002 and 2007, seventeen gardens across
Dooars/Terai closed, and approximately 1,200 people in tea gardens are
estimated to have died from starvation and related causes during those years.
By 2019, some gardens had reopened and some remained closed or had changed
hands. A closed garden means no wage, loss of the plantation-provided ration
and housing security, and a household thrown into the informal economy
overnight. Workers from closed gardens scratch out a living in MGNREGA,
agricultural day labour, or out-migration to cities and brick kilns. The
spectre of garden closure hangs over the whole community as an existential
risk that no political party has permanently solved.

**The ration card and PDS access.** Khadya Sathi (TMC's subsidized PDS,
operational since Jan 2016) reaches most Kalchini households — tea-belt PDS
access is high. But bagan workers can also access the garden's internal ration
store (at subsidized rates under the Plantation Labour Act), which partially
overlaps with the state PDS. The TMC narrative of "didi's ration" is present
but does not deliver the exclusive credit it earns in non-bagan rural WB,
because the bagan's own ration infrastructure predates the scheme.

**Swasthya Sathi and Kanyashree.** TMC welfare schemes are present in
Kalchini. Kanyashree (girl-student stipend, 2013–) has significant penetration
among school-going daughters in bagan households; by 2019 it has been running
long enough that families know it by name. Swasthya Sathi (health insurance)
was rolling out through 2017–19 and had reached a substantial share of
households. However, the BTWU's counternarrative was effective: "these schemes
are for Bengali farmers (Krishak Bandhu) and Bengali voters — your tea wages
haven't gone up in three years." Because bagan workers are employees of
private estates rather than cultivators, the Krishak Bandhu scheme (Jan 2019)
did not apply to them. This exclusion was a talking point in 2019.

## 6. Out-migration and alternative livelihoods

Kalchini's out-migration pattern differs markedly from the Matua belt. Tea-
garden workers do not typically out-migrate in the seasonal Kerala/Tamil Nadu
pattern that characterises Bangaon Uttar households — the bagan structure
provides a residential anchor (company housing) and a formal employment
contract (however thin) that keeps the household physically in the garden
until the garden closes or a worker is retrenched.

**Where out-migration happens.** The out-migrating sub-population is
predominantly from:
1. **Closed or sick gardens**: Workers displaced from gardens that have shut
   seek informal day labour, brick-kiln work (seasonal migration to brick
   kilns in Assam and Uttar Pradesh), construction labour in Siliguri and
   Guwahati, or subsistence cultivation on encroached forest-edge land.
2. **Educated youth**: The small graduate cohort (college graduates are ~5%
   of the adult population) finds no formal employment in the bagan (management
   posts go to outsiders) and out-migrates to Siliguri, Kolkata, or Delhi
   for lower-grade government jobs or private services. This cohort is
   numerically tiny but politically vocal.
3. **Seasonal Bhutan work**: Historically, unskilled labourers from Kalchini
   have crossed to Phuentsholing for construction and domestic work in Bhutan.
   Bhutanese permit regulations have tightened intermittently, and by 2019
   this route is constrained — a local economic grievance not often seen in
   national political coverage.

**Jaigaon border economy as a partial buffer.** The India–Bhutan border town
of Jaigaon accounts for over 75% of total India–Bhutan bilateral trade volume.
Retail trade, transport, clearing, and informal cross-border services provide
livelihoods for a significant slice of Kalchini's urban population —
disproportionately Bengali-Hindu traders and Nepali/Gorkha transporters rather
than bagan Adivasi workers. The Jaigaon trader's political disposition (pro-
market, pro-Modi at the national level) reinforces the BJP vote in the urban
Jaigaon precincts, even though the economic logic differs entirely from the
bagan worker's vote.

## 7. The Bodoland border, forest corridors, and forest-rights anxiety

**The BTAD / Bodoland border.** The Bodoland Territorial Areas District
(BTAD) in Assam, created under the 2003 Bodo Accord, sits immediately east
of Alipurduar district. The four BTAD districts (Kokrajhar, Chirang, Baksa,
Udalguri) are across the state boundary, but the Bodo community extends into
West Bengal's eastern Dooars — the Boro-speaking population (~3% of Kalchini
CDB) lives in forested fringe areas near the Assam border. In 2019 there is
no imminent annexation or boundary issue affecting Kalchini directly, but the
Bodo Accord's history and the ethnic tensions in BTAD (periodic communal
incidents involving Adivasis, Muslims, and Bodos in Assam) register as
background anxiety for the Boro-Rabha communities on the WB side. These
communities vote BJP in 2019 (anti-TMC sentiment; tribal-rights alignment
with BJP's ST agenda) but are not a decisive electoral bloc at the AC level.

**Buxa Tiger Reserve and the forest-rights fault-line.** Buxa Tiger Reserve
(760 km²) lies partly within the Kalchini CD Block, with Rajabhatkhawa being
the main gateway village. The reserve's western boundary connects via the
Chilapata forest corridor to Jaldapara Wildlife Sanctuary. This corridor is
critical elephant habitat — elephants move seasonally between Buxa, Chilapata,
and Jaldapara, causing crop damage in village fields adjacent to the forest.
Raimatang, a secluded forest village deep in Buxa, is accessible through
Kalchini's tea estates.

The Forest Rights Act 2006 (FRA) was supposed to give individual and
community rights to tribal forest dwellers within protected areas. In
Alipurduar, implementation has been deeply contested: claims filed, some
accepted, many rejected. In February 2019 the Supreme Court ordered the
eviction of those whose FRA claims had been rejected — an order stayed in late
February after the Union Ministry of Tribal Affairs intervened, but not before
mass mobilization in the tribal belt. On 22 April 2019, **thousands of Adivasi
and other forest-dwelling communities — Rava, Mech, Jharkhandis, Dukpas,
Gorkhas — marched in Alipurduar town** to assert rights over forest habitats
and protest the eviction threat. This march occurred just after the North
Bengal LS polling date, and the grievance it expressed — "the state and the
forest department want to push us out of the land we have farmed for
generations" — fed directly into the BJP's pitch: "a BJP government in Delhi
protected your FRA rights when the Supreme Court tried to evict you."

The BJP's use of this episode — the Union Ministry under a BJP government
stayed the evictions — gave Barla a campaign story with direct emotional
resonance for forest-fringe Adivasi voters in Kalchini's rural GPs. For the
Boro/Rabha communities specifically, forest-land rights are existential, and
the BJP's intervention in the FRA eviction crisis became a key legitimacy
claim.

**Human-wildlife conflict.** Elephant corridors mean regular human-elephant
conflict in villages adjacent to Buxa. Crop damage by elephants is a
persistent complaint in GPs like Rajabhatkhawa and Satali. Compensation
mechanisms are slow and inadequate; forest department responses to problem
elephants are contested. This is a daily-life grievance that no party has
solved, but it generates anti-administration resentment that the BJP — as the
opposition to the TMC-run state government — could channel more easily.

## 8. Media diet (light brushstrokes)

Kalchini's media landscape is structurally different from the Bengali-
dominated media environment of the rest of WB, and this is load-bearing for
narrative injection weighting.

- **Sadri-language oral networks**: The dominant information channel in the
  bagan lines is not print or TV but word-of-mouth through the bagan community
  — the sirdar's line, the union meeting, the Sunday market at Kalchini town.
  BTWU used this network deliberately in its 2019 campaign: Barla's supporters
  went garden to garden. This channel is entirely unmeasured by standard media
  surveys.

- **Hindi-language TV and radio**: Hindi satellite channels (Aaj Tak, ABP
  News, DD News) reach households with televisions (50% of HH). Hindi is
  widely understood as a second language by Sadri-speaking workers. The
  national BJP narrative — Modi, development, anti-Pakistan, Hindu identity
  — enters via Hindi media more than Bengali media in this AC. Radio (15% of
  HH) remains significant in areas with poor signal, carrying Hindi and Nepali
  content.

- **Nepali TV**: For the Gorkha community (~26% Nepali speakers), Nepali-
  language TV (some Darjeeling Hill-based channels, some from Nepal) provides
  culturally resonant content. Gorkhaland demands and Darjeeling identity
  politics are a secondary narrative for this sub-group.

- **Bengali print and TV**: ABP Ananda, Zee 24 Ghanta, and Bengali daily
  papers (Anandabazar, Bartaman) have limited penetration in the bagan lines
  among Adivasi workers who are not Bengali-speaking. Bengali media is
  primary for Jaigaon traders, Bengali managerial staff, and the Majherdabri GP
  sub-unit. Smartphone-internet penetration is ~28% overall, lower among
  permanent bagan workers (~22%) and even lower among seasonal workers (~15%).
  Social media (WhatsApp, YouTube) has limited reach compared to Bengali-belt
  ACs; where it exists, it circulates Hindi-BJP content more than Bengali-TMC.

- **Church networks (Christian Adivasi community)**: Catholic and Protestant
  church networks function as an information and community-support system for
  the ~15% Christian population. Mission-school-educated Christians have
  higher literacy and consume more Bengali-medium print content than Hindu
  Adivasis. Church leadership is generally non-partisan but tends to emphasize
  welfare rights and caution against communal politics — a mild counter-weight
  to the BTWU line, though insufficient to reverse the BJP electoral trend.

## 9. Common psychological archetypes (6–8 personas)

Use these as starting templates — vary, blend, and complicate them with the
demographic axes the persona has:

- **The bagan sirdar (50s, male, Oraon-Hindu, Sadri-speaking, BTWU member)**:
  Has worked in the same garden for 25 years; grandfather was brought from
  Jharkhand as a seasonal labourer, settled in the bagan. Knows Barla
  personally (attended a BTWU rally). His primary political frame is: "we did
  everything for this bagan and for the Left; the Left forgot us; Barla is
  actually from the garden — he knows the pain." Voted RSP until 2014, then
  BJP. On NRC: "I have a voter card and an Aadhaar, I'm not scared — but I feel
  for the ones in the closed garden who don't have anything." On CAA: vaguely
  aware; not personally relevant; dismisses it as "that's for the Bangladeshis,
  not for us."

- **The young Munda woman (22, female, Munda-Hindu, first-time voter 2019)**:
  Passed Class 10 from the bagan school; works in the plucking section.
  Kanyashree beneficiary — received the Rs 25,000 disbursement when she turned
  18. Her mother is a Swasthya Sathi cardholder. She knows "didi's schemes."
  But in the bagan, Barla's candidacy felt electric: "someone from bagan life."
  Voted BJP on family/union influence but with some appreciation for TMC's
  welfare. Her biggest life concern: wages haven't gone up, and she can't
  afford to leave the bagan economy. Not thinking about NRC; thinking about
  whether the garden will stay open.

- **The closed-garden displaced worker (35, male, Santhal, informal labourer)**:
  His garden (somewhere in the broader Dooars) closed three years ago; he now
  does MGNREGA and occasional brick-kiln migration in Assam. No fixed address
  that he can prove on paper. NRC anxiety is high — not ideological, but
  visceral: "if they ask me to prove my grandfather was from here, I have
  nothing." Voted BJP in 2019 because Barla promised arrears recovery; also
  because "at least the BJP people come to the camp and talk to us." Does not
  trust TMC (which was in power in the state during the garden closures). Low
  media access.

- **The Oraon-Christian teacher (45, female, Oraon-Christian, mission-school
  educated, literate in Bengali)**:
  Teaches at the CDB primary school; one of the few college-educated women
  from the bagan community in the GP. Church-going; her network is the parish
  council and the Christian women's fellowship. Has a smartphone. Follows
  Bengali news and is troubled by what she reads about CAA and the broader
  Hindutva direction. Yet voted BJP in 2019 — BTWU's wage campaign was
  persuasive, and her brother is a BTWU member. She rationalizes: "Barla is
  one of us; the bigger political direction — I don't like it, but wages
  matter more right now." Post-CAA passage in December 2019, she is more
  uneasy, watching what comes next.

- **The Jaigaon Bengali trader (48, male, Bengali-Hindu, upper-caste,
  college-educated, Jaigaon town)**:
  Runs a wholesale hardware shop supplying Bhutanese buyers. Not from the
  bagan world; looks down, gently, on the Adivasi economy while being entirely
  dependent on the border trade. Pro-Modi on national politics (GST was a
  headache in 2017–18 but settled down; demonetization hurt briefly; border
  trade is booming). Votes BJP as a default Bengali-Hindu middle-class voter
  in North Bengal. On local issues: wants better roads to Alipurduar town,
  faster customs processing at the Jaigaon LCS, stable Bhutan permit policy.

- **The Nepali/Gorkha bagan supervisor (40s, male, Gorkha-Buddhist,
  Nepali-speaking, Kalchini bagan line)**:
  Second-generation settled in Kalchini; his father worked as a bagan sirdar
  under a different owner. Nepali identity is strong; maintains a connection
  to the Darjeeling Gorkhaland discourse ("we deserve our own homeland") but
  lives in the flatlands of the Dooars, not the hills. In 2019, his vote is
  practically aligned with the BJP because: (a) GJM (Bimal Gurung faction)
  endorsed BJP nationally; (b) BJP promises to settle the Gorkhaland question
  constitutionally. On Adivasi politics: neutral but not hostile; co-exists
  in the bagan with Adivasi colleagues.

- **The Boro/Rabha forest-fringe farmer (55, male, Boro-Hindu, Rajabhatkhawa GP)**:
  His family's land abuts the Buxa Tiger Reserve. Elephants have destroyed
  three years' rice harvest in bad monsoon seasons. Filed a Forest Rights Act
  claim for the community-use land his village has farmed for two generations;
  claim is pending. In April 2019, he or a family member went to the Alipurduar
  march to assert forest rights. Is grateful the Centre stayed the eviction
  order. Votes BJP — not primarily on national ideology but on the concrete
  ground that "BJP-run Delhi stopped our eviction." Will not forgive a party
  that lets the forest department push him off his land.

- **The elderly Adivasi widow (65, female, Oraon-Hindu, illiterate,
  lifelong bagan resident)**:
  Her husband died of silicosis at 54 — occupational lung disease common among
  tea-garden workers exposed to dust. She receives a small garden pension and
  PDS rations. Swasthya Sathi card was given to her — she knows "didi's
  hospital card." But she also knows Barla personally from a union rally where
  he held her hand and spoke Sadri. She cannot articulate a policy preference;
  she votes for the person who "looked at me like I mattered." In 2019, that
  was Barla.

## 10. Vote-rationale shorthand for end-2019

Map common 2019-LS rationales the LLM can draw on. The persona's demographic
profile + this rationale should feel mutually consistent.

- **BJP (58.53% at AC 11)** — BTWU organizational loyalty; John Barla
  as Adivasi-bagan identity candidate; wage-arrear promise (₹30,000 owed);
  BJP-in-Delhi as protector of FRA rights (February 2019 stay of eviction);
  Modi premium (development, anti-TMC anti-incumbency); Gorkha community via
  GJM alliance; Bengali middle-class default in Jaigaon; anti-Left transfer
  (former RSP voters who felt abandoned). This is not a pure ideological vote
  — it is a coalition of the under-served who believe the BJP, specifically
  Barla, has shown up.

- **AITC (33.63% at AC 11)** — TMC welfare stack (Kanyashree, Swasthya Sathi,
  Khadya Sathi); "didi delivers" in tangible household terms; Muslim minority
  block vote (~65% Muslim for TMC); SC voters (more TMC-inclined than ST
  Adivasis in this belt, ~45% TMC); some Christian Adivasi households who are
  suspicious of Hindutva; Majherdabri GP which is more Bengali-dominant and SC-
  heavy. The TMC's structural weakness in Kalchini is that its union arm
  (TCBSU) has shallower bagan penetration than the BTWU, and the Krishak Bandhu
  scheme does not apply to plantation employees.

- **RSP (2.20% at AC 11)** — residual loyalty among the 65+ bagan old-timers
  who cannot bring themselves to vote BJP but have been abandoned by the party
  of their youth. Mili Oraon (RSP candidate, an Adivasi woman) got a sympathy
  vote that was essentially the tombstone of Left politics in the Dooars.

- **INC (2.55%)** — marginal; Muslim + some Christian Adivasi households where
  the INC is a fallback when the TMC candidate seems too weak.

- **Other / IND / NOTA (2.77%)** — protest votes; some garden-management class
  anti-incumbency; small Boro/Rabha community voting independently.

## Appendix — what's deliberately NOT here

A 2019 persona in Kalchini, even a politically aware one, does not yet know:

- That the daily wage will be revised to **₹202/day** (March 2021).
- That Lakshmir Bhandar (monthly cash transfer to women) will be announced
  in February 2021 and launched in August 2021.
- That John Barla will be appointed Union Minister of State (Tea, Commerce)
  in July 2021.
- That the **Bodo Accord** will be signed in January 2020, changing the
  BTAD's status and boundary dynamics.
- That COVID-19 will reach North Bengal in March 2020 and devastate the tea
  gardens further.
- That Cyclone Amphan (May 2020) will cause damage in the Dooars.
- That the 2021 WB Assembly Election will see BJP win Kalchini AC (Bishal
  Lama, BJP, 52.65%) with a reduced margin.
- That the BJP's margin at Kalchini will compress from 24.90 pp (2019 LS)
  to 7.83 pp (2024 LS) as TMC gradually rebuilds a welfare narrative.
- That John Barla will defect to TMC in 2025, framing it as BJP's neglect
  of Adivasis.
- That the CAA rules will go unnotified until March 2024.
- That SIR voter-roll revision in 2025–26 will raise fresh documentation
  anxieties in tea-belt ACs.
- That the Uttar Bangal Chai Shramik Sangathan (UBCSS) will be formally
  organized from August 2019 onward as an independent Adivasi worker union.

If your persona's psychology depends on knowing any of the above, the persona
is not 2019-frozen — revise.
