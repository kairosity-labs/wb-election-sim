## §1 Who lives here

**AC 222 Jhargram** sits at the heart of the Jangalmahal region — the contiguous
forest belt of West Bengal's southwest, where the Sal (Shorea robusta) and dry
deciduous forest merges into the eastern fringe of the Chota Nagpur Plateau. The
constituency covers Jhargram Municipality (the town that is now the district
headquarters), plus the Gram Panchayats of Bandhgora, Manikpara, Radhanagar, and
Sapdhara from Jhargram and Binpur I community development blocks. Total electorate
roughly 1.84 lakh (2019).

**The new district context.** On 4 April 2017, Jhargram became West Bengal's 22nd
district, carved out of Paschim Medinipur. This was a long-standing demand of the
tribal communities of Jangalmahal who felt perpetually subordinated to the political
economy of Midnapore town. The creation of the district was a TMC administration
initiative and was initially welcomed, though by 2018-19 many voters felt the new
district machinery had simply replicated the same patronage networks — TMC block
leaders continued to control scheme delivery.

**Tribal demography — the defining fact.** Scheduled Tribes constitute approximately
29.4% of Jhargram district's population (2011 census, reconstituted boundaries). The
dominant ST communities in and around the AC are:

- **Santali (Santhali)**: The largest tribal group in Jangalmahal, speakers of
  Santali language (Austro-Asiatic, Munda family), with its own script, Ol Chiki,
  developed by Pandit Raghunath Murmu in the 1920s. In Jhargram district, Santali
  speakers form ~18.7% of the total population. The Santhals are concentrated in
  the forest-adjacent villages and GP areas; the Jhargram municipality itself has a
  more mixed population.

- **Munda**: Another significant Munda-family tribal group, with Mundari speakers
  constituting ~1.45% of the district population. Many Munda families hold
  hereditary claims to specific forest tracts.

- **Ho, Bhumij, Lodha**: Smaller but distinct communities. The Lodha (also called
  Kheria Sabar) are a particularly marginalised primitive tribal group (now termed
  Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Group, PVTG) with deep historical association with
  this forest zone.

- **Kurmi-Mahato** (Other Backward Class): The Kurmi or Mahato community — not ST
  classified but OBC — forms a substantial segment (~40%+ in parts of the broader
  Jangalmahal belt, though their share varies by AC). In Jhargram AC specifically,
  Kurmis are politically significant and carry a separate, restive identity — they
  have long demanded ST status, arguing historical and cultural affinity with
  scheduled tribes.

**Scheduled Castes.** SCs constitute ~20.1% of Jhargram district, including
communities like Bagdi, Bauri, Dom, and Hari. They are present in the Jhargram
municipality and surrounding GPs.

**Bengali Hindu** (non-tribal, non-SC, general category): A minority in the rural
hinterland but proportionally larger in Jhargram town. Many work in government
service, petty trade, and education. Their political behavior has historically
aligned with whoever controls patronage — CPM for decades, then TMC from 2011.

**Muslim population**: Near-zero in most of the Jangalmahal heartland. Jhargram
district has ~2.46% Muslim population overall, concentrated in specific pockets near
the Garbeta belt, not the core Jhargram AC area. This demographic fact is
politically significant: unlike eastern Bengal districts where Hindu-Muslim tension
shapes BJP rhetoric, in Jangalmahal the BJP found it necessary to build a different
mobilisation frame — tribal Hindu identity vs. "illegal infiltrator" threat was
deployed with reference to Bangladeshi migrants in distant districts, not a local
reality.

**Sarna religion.** In the 2011 census, 9.83% of Jhargram district's population
recorded their religion as "Sarna" or related nature-worship practice. This is a
substantial undercount by most estimates — many Santhals and Mundas who practice
their indigenous animist-naturist religion (worshipping the sacred grove or Jaher
Than, revering Marang Buru/Great Mountain God, ancestral spirits) were miscoded as
Hindu. The movement for a separate Sarna religious code in the census — distinct from
Hindu, Christian, and other categories — was an active political demand going into
2019, and a fault line between communities inclined toward BJP's Hindu-incorporation
frame and those asserting a non-Hindu Adivasi identity.

**Language and media note.** Bengali is the first language of ~78.2% of district
residents, but in the forest hamlets, Santali (with Ol Chiki literacy among younger
people who attended Santhali-medium primary schools) and Mundari are the home
languages. Bilingualism is common but not universal: older forest-dwelling women
often have limited Bengali literacy.

## §2 The Maoist memory

**2008 — the Salboni blast and its aftermath.** On 2 November 2008, CPI (Maoist)
militants detonated a land mine targeting the convoy of Chief Minister Buddhadeb
Bhattacharya near Salboni, Paschim Medinipur. The attack killed no politicians but
injured six policemen. The state police response was indiscriminate: mass raids,
arbitrary arrests, and alleged beatings of tribal youth in the Lalgarh block of
present-day Jhargram district. This police brutality lit the fuse.

**The Lalgarh Movement (November 2008 – mid-2009).** A spontaneous uprising of
tribal communities, primarily Santhals, Mundas, and Lodhas, coalesced under the
**People's Committee Against Police Atrocities (PCAPA)**, led by Chhatradhar
Mahato, a Kurmi community activist from Lalgarh. The PCAPA's eleven demands included
compensation for police violence, removal of paramilitary camps, and an end to
arbitrary detention. For months, large forest tracts were effectively off-limits to
police and government. Women organized road blockades — images of tribal women with
lathis and dhols became iconic. The CPI (Maoist), with their own programme and armed
cadres, operated in parallel and provided ideological framing, but early PCAPA
activity was genuinely grassroots.

By early 2009, the Maoists had co-opted significant parts of the movement. They
attacked CPM offices, eliminated party cadres, and declared "liberated zones" in
parts of Lalgarh, Jhargram, Binpur, and Gopiballavpur. The CPM's armed militia,
the **Harmad Bahini**, retaliated with vigilante violence, deepening the bloodshed.
Between 2008 and 2011, the Jangalmahal recorded approximately 355 civilian deaths
and 53 security force deaths from Maoist-related violence, making it among the
bloodiest subnational conflicts in contemporary India.

**Operation Lalgarh (June 2009).** The CPM state government requisitioned central
forces. On 20 June 2009, CRPF, COBRA (Commando Battalion for Resolute Action), BSF
(deployed here for the first time in an anti-Naxal operation), and state police
launched a joint combing operation in Lalgarh, beginning with the Jhitkha jungle.
The forces faced human shields of 2,000+ villagers at entry points; tear gas and
lathi charges dispersed crowds. The operation took three days to reach the Lalgarh
police station.

**Operation Green Hunt (October 2009 onward).** The Union Home Ministry launched
the wider Operation Green Hunt across the "red corridor" states. In Jangalmahal,
this meant sustained counter-insurgency operations — helicopter surveillance,
nighttime raids, encounters, and alleged fake encounters. The period 2009-2011 saw
peak Maoist violence (425 incidents in 2010) as well as peak security force
deployment.

**The Mamata factor — pre-2011.** Mamata Banerjee, in opposition as TMC leader,
cultivated the Jangalmahal movement as a political opportunity. She visited tribal
leaders, shared stages with Chhatradhar Mahato before his arrest, and framed the
entire conflict as CPM state terror on innocent tribals. She promised: if voted to
power, she would address tribal grievances. Her party's 2011 election campaign
in Jangalmahal ran almost entirely on the anti-CPM/anti-Harmad Bahini sentiment.
This posture built TMC massive goodwill in the region.

**Kishenji killed (24 November 2011).** Mallojula Koteswara Rao, known as "Kishenji,"
was the CPI (Maoist) Politburo member and the most visible Maoist leader in the
Jangalmahal-Odisha-Andhra corridor. He was killed in Burishol forest, Paschim
Midnapore, in an operation by COBRA and CRPF. The official version: a 30-minute
firefight. Disputed accounts: activists who examined the body found evidence of
torture — burns, knife injuries, cut marks — suggesting a staged encounter after
capture. The controversy deepened tribal communities' suspicion of state violence.
By late 2011, however, Maoist capability in Jangalmahal had declined sharply.
Kishenji's death, combined with Mamata's ascension to power in May 2011 (which
changed the political incentives for tribal support), effectively ended the
high-intensity phase of the insurgency.

**The CPM vilified.** The legacy of the Harmad Bahini, the arbitrary arrests, and
the mass graves of the CPM period permanently shattered the party's tribal base.
CPM district secretary Pradip Sarkar claimed 450 party leaders and workers were
killed by the combined forces of TMC and Maoists after 2011. By 2014, CPM had
virtually no organizational presence in most forest-bloc panchayats of Jhargram.

**What the Maoist memory means in 2019.** For tribal voters in Jhargram AC and its
surrounding belt, the 2008-2011 period is lived memory, not history. Key residues:

1. **Deep anti-police consciousness.** Any state force — even under a different
   government — carries the shadow of the 2009 CRPF operations. Voter distrust of
   government authority is reflexive among older Santhali and Munda communities.

2. **Anti-CPM as permanent identity.** Voting CPM is essentially impossible for most
   tribal voters. The CPM's role in the Harmad killings is not forgotten.

3. **Chhatradhar Mahato as a semi-mythic figure.** He spent 2009-2019 in jail on
   UAPA charges. By 2019, with his release approaching (he was let out in August
   2019), his figure carried symbolic weight — a tribal man jailed for speaking
   against police atrocities. His eventual TMC alignment (post-2019, out of scope)
   was not yet public knowledge in the 2019 election season.

4. **The ambivalence about Mamata.** She came to power promising redemption. Some
   of that goodwill persisted in 2016 (TMC won Jhargram AC with ~54.9% in the
   2016 assembly election). But by 2019, a segment of tribal voters who had backed
   TMC were now asking: what changed?

## §3 The 2019 political earthquake

**The Jhargram Lok Sabha constituency** (reserved for Scheduled Tribes) comprises
seven assembly segments: Nayagram (ST), Gopiballavpur, Jhargram (AC 222), Binpur
(ST), Garbeta, Salboni (in Paschim Medinipur), and Bandwan (in Purulia). The 2019
LS result thus reflects the broader Jangalmahal belt, not Jhargram AC alone — but
the AC-222 result fed into this aggregate.

**2019 Lok Sabha results (Jhargram LS constituency):**
- **Kunar Hembram (BJP)**: 6,26,583 votes — **44.56%** — WON
- **Birbaha Saren (AITC/TMC)**: 6,14,816 votes — **43.72%** — Lost by 11,767
- Margin: 11,767 votes (very narrow)
- CPM: ~5.38%
- INC: ~1.48%

Birbaha Saren was a Santhali woman from the tribal community — TMC's attempt to
field a tribal face. Kunar Hembram is an IIT engineer from a tribal background who
represented BJP's projection of tribal meritocracy + central government patronage.

**Context for the swing.** In 2014, TMC's Uma Saren had won the same seat with
54.60%. The BJP's gain of ~25 percentage points in five years — moving from distant
third to narrow first — represents one of the most dramatic swings in any ST
constituency in West Bengal. The factors driving this swing:

**A. Anti-cut-money anger.** TMC's welfare delivery system by 2018-19 had become
entangled with a system of informal levies known as "cut money" — where local
TMC panchayat leaders and block functionaries extracted a share of every government
payment, from MGNREGA wages to housing scheme instalments to forest land title fees.
Beneficiaries consistently reported losing 20-35% of their entitlements to cut money.
For tribal households earning Rs 6,000-10,000 from MGNREGA annually, a Rs 2,000
deduction was substantively impoverishing. This was not a BJP allegation alone — it
was verified by ground reporters. The BJP successfully ran "cut money" as its central
anti-TMC campaign message in Jangalmahal.

**B. The forest rights promise vs. reality.** The Forest Rights Act (2006) entitled
tribal forest dwellers to individual and community land titles (pattas). By end-2018,
Jhargram district had distributed individual forest right pattas to 14,594
households covering ~3,508 acres. But the process was plagued by:
- Gram sabha meetings being skipped or conducted without proper quorum
- Forest department interference prioritising plantation contracts over community
  rights
- Claims rejected on technical grounds without appeal mechanisms
- TMC-affiliated middlemen inserting themselves into the process and extracting fees
The BJP's tribal outreach promised to "honour Jal, Jangal, Jameen" (water, forest,
land) — the three foundational demands of Adivasi movements nationwide.

**C. Sarna code as political lightning rod.** The demand for a separate Sarna
religious code in the census — so that tribals practicing their indigenous animist
religion could be counted separately from Hindus — was an active mobilisation issue.
The BJP's actual position was ambivalent: the RSS and VKA preferred tribal
communities to be counted as Hindus, while BJP politicians at the state level made
vague promises about considering the demand to win votes. TMC had already made
some gestures — officially recognising Ol Chiki script, introducing Santhali-medium
primary schools — but had not delivered on the census demand. For Sarna activists,
neither party was fully trustworthy; the BJP's slipperiness on the issue was masked
by its general anti-TMC momentum.

**D. BJP's Jangalmahal sweep.** BJP won all five Lok Sabha constituencies in the
Jangalmahal belt in 2019 (Jhargram, Bankura, Bishnupur, Purulia, Medinipur). The
Jhargram margin was the narrowest — suggesting Jhargram AC itself was competitive
between the two parties, with TMC still holding some ground in the municipality
and in the Santhali-majority GPs.

**The AC-222 specific dynamic.** The sitting MLA was Sukumar Hansda (TMC), who had
won the 2016 assembly election comfortably (~54.9% vs Jharkhand Party's ~24.4%).
Sukumar Hansda was also West Bengal Legislative Assembly Deputy Speaker — giving
the seat some incumbent advantage. However, by 2019 LS, the panchayat layer
(block-level TMC organisation) had generated significant cut money grievances.
Estimates suggest BJP led in the Jhargram AC segment during the 2019 LS count,
consistent with the overall Jangalmahal swing.

## §4 Forest economy and PESA rights

**The primacy of the forest.** Jhargram district's economy is inextricably linked
to its forests. The dominant tree species is **Sal (Shorea robusta)**, which
constitutes the backbone of the dry deciduous forest covering large swathes of
the district. The economic life of forest-dependent tribal households revolves
around a seasonal calendar of forest produce collection:

**Key NTFPs (Non-Timber Forest Products):**

- **Sal leaves (Shorea robusta patra):** Collected daily and year-round by women
  and children, stitched into leaf plates (pattal) sold in weekly markets. A
  primary income source for the poorest households, earning Rs 20-50 per day in
  a good season. Pattal and dona (cups) made from sal leaves supply the entire
  Bengali roadside food culture.

- **Kendu/Tendu leaf (Diospyros melanoxylon):** The "green gold" of Jangalmahal.
  Tendu leaves are used as the outer wrapper for bidis (the hand-rolled cigarette
  that dominates tobacco consumption among India's poor). Collection season is
  May-June, immediately before the monsoon. The trade is lucrative but controlled:
  in many states, tendu leaf is a government monopoly. In West Bengal, the Forest
  Department has historically controlled the contracting, which has been a source
  of patronage and corruption — the contracts go to politically connected
  contractors who pay below-market rates to collectors. BJP campaign material in
  2019 highlighted this as a TMC-contractor nexus.

- **Mahua (Madhuca longifolia) flowers:** Collected in April-May, Mahua flowers
  are used to make alcohol (used both in tribal rituals and as a poor household
  income source), and have nutritional and medicinal value. The seasonal collection
  of Mahua flowers is among the most important income events for poor Santhali and
  Munda households.

- **Bamboo:** Used for construction, weaving, and sold as raw material to handicraft
  industries. Joint Forest Management committees in Jhargram have plantation
  programmes for bamboo.

- **Honey, medicinal plants, mushrooms, wild fruits:** Supplementary income and
  household nutrition, particularly important in the months before the Kharif harvest.

**Joint Forest Management (JFM).** West Bengal was a pioneer in JFM — the Arabari
experiment of the 1970s-80s in adjacent Midnapore district was the model that
influenced national forest policy. By 2019, Jhargram district had approximately
480 Village Forest Protection Committees (VFPCs) under the JFM programme, covering
large tracts of forest. In theory, these committees give tribal communities a
share of forest revenues and management authority. In practice, the programme had
significant distortions:
- VFPCs were often dominated by the politically connected (TMC panchayat leaders)
  rather than the most forest-dependent households
- Revenue sharing from timber sales and other contracts frequently did not reach
  individual member households
- Women — who are the primary NTFP collectors — were formally represented but
  practically excluded from decision-making

**Forest Rights Act (FRA) 2006 — implementation deficit.** The Scheduled Tribes
and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (Recognition of Forest Rights) Act, 2006,
theoretically gives forest-dwelling communities the right to:
(a) Individual forest land titles for land cultivated/occupied before December 2005
(b) Community forest rights over forest areas traditionally used by a village
(c) Right to protect and manage community forest resources

Implementation in Jhargram:
- By October 2018, ~14,594 individual forest rights (IFR) pattas distributed,
  covering ~3,508 acres, and 72 Community Forest Rights (CFR) covering ~42.62 acres
- These numbers represent a fraction of eligible claimants — West Bengal's
  overall FRA performance was rated poorly by rights organisations
- Critical complaints: gram sabha processes were largely bypassed; the Forest
  Department acted as gatekeeper rather than facilitator; many legitimate claims
  were rejected citing lack of documentary proof — a Kafkaesque requirement for
  families who have no documents precisely because the state never recognised them
- Title deeds (pattas), when issued, were sometimes for land already encroached upon
  by plantation companies or project developers

**PESA Act (Panchayats Extension to Scheduled Areas, 1996).** This Act, passed by
Parliament, required state governments to extend democratic self-governance
(panchayati raj) to Schedule V tribal areas, with special protections for gram
sabha authority over land, resources, and local decisions. **West Bengal never
notified PESA.** Jhargram and the Jangalmahal belt are not covered under Schedule V
(unlike Jharkhand, Odisha, Chhattisgarh). This is a structural governance gap that
tribal rights organisations have repeatedly raised — the gram sabhas in Jhargram
do not have the same empowered role over forest resources that PESA would require.

**Forest contractors and TMC middlemen.** By 2018-19, a well-documented pattern
had emerged: TMC-affiliated contractors controlled the kendu leaf collection
contracts, sal leaf depot licensing, and bamboo extraction permissions. Tribal
collectors were paid below market rates; complaints to the Forest Department went
unheard because the local Forest Beat Officers were aligned with the same political
network. This "contractor-politician-Forest Department" nexus was a tangible
grievance that BJP fieldworkers exploited systematically in their 2019 campaign.

## §5 Welfare vs. identity

**TMC's welfare architecture in Jangalmahal.** After coming to power in 2011,
Mamata Banerjee's government rolled out targeted welfare schemes with specific
provisions for Jangalmahal:

- **Gitanjali Housing Scheme (2011):** Subsidised housing providing Rs 75,000 for
  construction to economically weaker households, with specific allocation focus
  on Jangalmahal, Sundarbans, and Hills. Visible impact in many villages.

- **MGNREGA (Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act):** Continued
  from the earlier UPA central programme. In Jhargram's forest villages, MGNREGA
  was often the only reliable cash income, providing 100 days of work at the
  prevailing wage (Rs 182-191/day in 2018-19 in West Bengal). Cut money on MGNREGA
  wages was the most commonly reported grievance.

- **Khadya Sathi (2016):** Food security scheme providing rice/wheat at Rs 2/kg.
  Jhargram's tribal households received the maximum allotment. TMC campaign material
  widely cited this.

- **Jai Johar Scheme:** Monthly financial assistance of Rs 1,000 to ST beneficiaries.
  Target demographic alignment with Jhargram's tribal electorate.

- **Bandhu Prakalpa:** Rs 1,000 monthly for SC beneficiaries.

- **Kanyashree Prakalpa (2013):** Conditional cash transfer for adolescent girls —
  Rs 750 annually for girls 13-18 in school, Rs 25,000 one-time payment at 18 if
  unmarried. This won the UN Public Service Award (2017) and was a genuine TMC
  success in reducing child marriage in tribal areas, where the practice had been
  prevalent.

- **Shilpi Kumari Scheme:** Rs 1,000/month for folk artists — directly relevant
  to Jangalmahal's Chhau dance, tribal music (dhol, madol), and weaving traditions.

**The delivery gap and its politics.** By 2018-19, the accumulated evidence from
ground reporting showed a consistent pattern: TMC's welfare schemes were real but
their last-mile delivery was captured by the party's panchayat machinery. The
practical experience of many tribal households was:
- Hearing about schemes from TMC politicians at rallies
- Being told by local panchayat member that they needed to "register" through
  the TMC booth committee
- Losing 20-30% of cash transfers to informal "processing fees"
- Watching non-tribal TMC-aligned contractors and businessmen visibly enrich
  themselves from the scheme ecosystem

This created a paradox: beneficiaries were grateful for the schemes in the abstract
(Kanyashree was genuinely transformative for girls' education) but felt humiliated
and cheated in the concrete transaction.

**Cultural marginalisation.** For the educated Santhali and Munda middle class
(teachers, NGO workers, Ol Chiki activists), TMC's approach to tribal identity
felt paternalistic. Key grievances:

- **Sarna vs. Hindu identity**: TMC's messaging regularly referred to tribals as
  part of Bengal's "Hindu" electorate, which many Sarna practitioners found
  erasing and offensive. The Ol Chiki script recognition was welcome, but Mamata's
  simultaneous rejection of the demand for a Sarna census code was seen as
  inconsistent.

- **Jhargram district — symbolic vs. substantive**: The 2017 district bifurcation
  was welcomed as symbolic recognition of the region's distinct identity. But the
  administrative machinery remained staffed by Bengali babu generalist IAS/WBS
  cadres from outside; tribal community leaders felt locked out of the new
  district bureaucracy.

- **Language in education**: While Santhali-medium primary schools had been set
  up in some blocks, the transition to secondary schooling was Bengali-only,
  creating a discontinuity that disadvantaged Santhali-mother-tongue students.
  Parents who wanted quality education for their children had to choose between
  tribal language instruction and academic competitiveness.

## §6 BJP's tribal mobilisation

**Long groundwork before the 2019 harvest.** The BJP's surge in Jangalmahal in
2019 was not spontaneous. It built on two decades of RSS ideological groundwork
through affiliates and front organisations:

**Akhil Bharatiya Vanvasi Kalyan Ashram (ABVKA / Vanvasi Kalyan Ashram).**
Founded in 1952, the VKA is the RSS's primary tribal outreach organisation. It has
been operational in West Bengal since 1978 — 12 of its 14 Bengal hostels were
established between 1978 and 1992, during the CPM period. In the Jangalmahal area
specifically, VKA operates:
- Student hostels providing free or subsidised accommodation for tribal students
  from forest villages attending town schools — addressing the education access gap
- Skill training centers (sewing, carpentry) framed as livelihood support
- Cultural programmes connecting tribal communities to broader Hindu ritual
  practices — Ram Navami celebrations, Hanuman temple puja, Saraswati Puja with
  Ol Chiki/tribal cultural elements incorporated

The VKA explicitly rejects the term "Adivasi" (original inhabitant) in favour of
"Vanvasi" (forest dweller), a terminological choice that carries political weight:
"Adivasi" asserts indigenous prior claim to land; "Vanvasi" simply describes where
people live, without implying prior ownership rights. This was contested by tribal
rights organisations but was generally unnoticed by ordinary voters.

**Ideological framing: Ghar Wapsi.** The VKA's long-term project in Jangalmahal
was to counter Christian missionary activity (the region had small but growing
Christian communities, particularly Santhals converted through Lutheran and Baptist
missions active since the 19th century) and bring "converted tribals back into the
Hindu fold." Ghar Wapsi (homecoming) ceremonies were organised across Bankura,
Purulia, and Jhargram. This was ideologically significant: it positioned the BJP/RSS
ecosystem as protecting tribal cultural authenticity from "foreign" (missionary)
influence, even as it simultaneously tried to incorporate tribal communities into
pan-Hindu identity.

**Saraswati Shishu Mandir network.** RSS-affiliated schools, named after Hindu
deities (Saraswati, Ma Sarada, Ramakrishna), operated in many Jangalmahal villages
and provided a touchpoint for BJP social outreach. These schools offered low-cost
English-medium or bilingual instruction with embedded value formation.

**Ram Navami and Hanuman Jayanti as mobilisation events.** From 2015-16 onward, BJP
and VHP organised large-scale Ram Navami processions in Jhargram town and adjacent
blocks — these events served as visible demonstrations of Hindutva presence and as
BJP-aligned social bonding occasions. Many tribal youth participated; the
festivities provided social contact and entertainment in areas with limited options.

**BJP's political messaging for tribal voters in 2019:**

1. **"Your forests, your rights":** BJP promised genuine implementation of FRA pattas,
   free of the TMC contractor-middleman layer. Modi government had also made promises
   (imperfectly implemented nationally) about expanding forest rights.

2. **Sarna code promise (ambiguous):** BJP politicians in Jangalmahal made vague
   verbal commitments that the Modi government "would consider" the Sarna code
   demand. This was never formalised — RSS/VKA opposed it — but the verbal signal
   was enough to keep Sarna activists uncertain and non-hostile.

3. **Anti-infiltration/Bangladeshi migrant rhetoric:** BJP's anti-Bangladeshi
   Muslim narrative resonated among tribal communities in Jhargram differently from
   Bengali Hindu communities. For Santhali and Munda voters, the concern was not
   primarily communal — it was about land. Forest land encroachment by non-tribal
   (and sometimes Muslim) migrants from Bangladesh was a real local grievance in
   some border-adjacent blocks. BJP effectively linked its national anti-NRC/anti-
   infiltration narrative to this local land rights concern.

4. **Tribal dignity and recognition:** Kumar Hembram being an IIT-educated ST
   candidate was symbolic — BJP was presenting tribal meritocracy, not just charity.
   This contrasted with the TMC's paternalistic "we have given you schemes" approach.

5. **Anti-cut-money:** Most effective message. Tribal voters knew exactly what it
   meant. They had experienced it directly.

**The Sarna-BJP contradiction.** The BJP's Hinduisation project was not universally
welcomed. A substantial segment of Jhargram's tribal population — particularly the
Sarna dharma activists, educated Ol Chiki proponents, and tribal rights NGO workers —
saw the VKA-BJP ecosystem as a threat to their distinct identity, not a protector of
it. For these voters, TMC's cultural recognition gestures (Santhali-medium schools,
Ol Chiki official recognition) were more meaningful than BJP's Hindu incorporation
project. But this segment was not large enough to prevent the 2019 swing.

## §7 Media diet and information environment

**The Jhargram AC information geography** is fragmented along language, geography,
and infrastructure lines.

**Print media:**
- *Anandabazar Patrika* (Bengali): Circulates in Jhargram town and among literate
  Bengali-speaking households. Political coverage broadly TMC-sympathetic in 2019
  period, though its reporting on Lalgarh/Jangalmahal has been extensive and
  relatively even-handed historically.
- *Ei Samay* (Bengali, Anandabazar group): Somewhat more urban-focused, limited
  penetration in forest villages.
- *Bartaman Patrika*, *Aajkaal*: Minor circulation in Jhargram town.
- **Santali-language newspapers and pamphlets:** Several small Santali-language
  publications operate from Jamshedpur and Ranchi (cross-border), circulating in
  Jhargram's Santhali-majority areas. These serve as primary reading material for
  Ol Chiki literate readers, who consume news about tribal rights, Sarna movement,
  and identity politics that Bengali-language media does not cover.

**Television:**
- Star Jalsha, Zee Bangla for entertainment (dominant in all households with TV)
- News channels: ABP Ananda (Bengali) and Zee 24 Ghanta (Bengali) are primary
  TV news sources in Jhargram town. Their 2019 coverage was heavily focused on
  the BJP-TMC confrontation at the state level.
- Limited national TV penetration in forest hamlet households without cable.

**Radio:**
- **Radio Milan (90.4 MHz):** Jhargram's community radio station — one of the few
  local-language information sources reaching forest villages. Broadcasts in Bengali
  and Santali, covering local agriculture, weather, health information, and cultural
  programmes. Trusted for practical information by rural listeners.
- AIR Midnapore station: Weather, agriculture advisory content.

**Mobile phones and WhatsApp (2018-19):**
- By 2019, mobile penetration had reached even remote forest villages, primarily
  through cheap 2G/3G feature phones and early smartphones.
- **WhatsApp forwards** in Santhali, Bengali, and mixed-language formats were an
  important political information channel in the 2018-19 period. BJP's digital
  teams, via Booth Committee WhatsApp groups, were systematically active.
  VKA-affiliated workers maintained group chats with tribal youth in hostels.
- TMC's digital outreach was less systematically organised at the booth level in
  Jangalmahal (stronger in urban West Bengal).
- Viral content: Morphed images of TMC corruption, inflammatory content about
  "Bengali Hindu" identity, and videos of CPM's Harmad Bahini violence (some
  old, some new, often decontextualised) circulated widely on WhatsApp.

**Santhali YouTube channels:** Small but growing ecosystem of Santali-language
content creators — folk songs, Ol Chiki tutorials, tribal rights commentary —
began to reach Santhali youth in 2018-19. These channels operated outside both
TMC and BJP information ecosystems, creating a vernacular public sphere for
tribal identity discussion.

**Tribal organisation pamphlets and word-of-mouth:** Village-level Sarna
organisations, tribal student unions (like the All Jharkhand Students Union, which
had some presence in West Bengal's Jangalmahal), and grassroots women's groups
(Mahila Mandal) were important information relays for the forest hamlet population
that had no TV or reliable mobile connectivity. The ward member or the VFPC
secretary often served as the primary news source.

**The PCAPA memory circulates.** Stories about Chhatradhar Mahato, Operation
Lalgarh, and the Jangalmahal uprising of 2008-2011 continued to circulate through
community memory and retelling. By 2019, a generation of 30-45 year olds had
personal memories of the movement — or parents who did. This institutional memory
shaped reception of BJP's anti-state, anti-CPM, anti-TMC messaging.

## §8 Psychological archetypes

The following personas represent the modal types within the Jhargram AC 2019
electorate. They are not exhaustive but capture the dominant psychological
configurations.

---

**Persona A: The young Santhali MGNREGA worker (female, 22-28)**

*Profile:* Santhali mother tongue, Ol Chiki literate (attended Santhali-medium
primary school, then Bengali-medium high school), married with one child. Lives
in a forest GP hamlet 15 km from Jhargram town. MGNREGA is primary income source
(~80-100 days/year). Also collects sal leaves, sells in weekly market.

*Political memory:* Too young to directly recall Lalgarh uprising but has heard
the stories from parents. Knows the Harmad Bahini as the bogeyman of the CPM era.
Received Kanyashree as a girl (benefited from the Rs 25,000 at age 18).

*2019 salience:* MGNREGA wages are cut — local panchayat leader deducts Rs 1,500-
2,000 from her annual entitlement as "processing fee." Kanyashree was good but
feels like the exception. Forest land title (patta) for her family's land was
"under process" for three years without resolution. Has heard BJP promises about
forest rights via WhatsApp voice message in Santali.

*Psychological orientation:* Ambivalent. TMC gave real benefits (Kanyashree).
TMC also takes cut money. BJP feels new and promising but also threatening to
her Sarna identity — the VKA hostel boys speak of tribal people as "Hindu Vanvasi"
and she finds this disconcerting. In 2019, likely wavered but slightly inclined BJP
given cut money anger.

---

**Persona B: The elderly Munda woman with forest land claim (female, 55-65)**

*Profile:* Mundari-speaking, minimal Bengali literacy, lives in a forest village
near Binpur I block. Has cultivated 2 acres of forest land for 30+ years. Son
submitted FRA claim; it was rejected on "insufficient evidence" grounds despite
neighbours with same land use having received pattas.

*Political memory:* Remembers Operation Lalgarh — CRPF camped in the village for
two weeks in 2009. Terrifying. Her cousin was beaten by Harmad cadres in 2008.
Deep distrust of all outside authority. Voted TMC in 2011 and 2014 because Mamata
didi came and spoke against police atrocities. Now disillusioned.

*2019 salience:* Her family's FRA claim rejection is the central political grievance.
The Forest Department's rejection letter came through the local TMC panchayat
contact — she cannot separate TMC from the rejection.

*Psychological orientation:* Withdrawn, cynical. May abstain, or vote BJP because
someone from the VKA hostel came to the village and explained (in simple terms) that
"Modi government will give pattas freely." Has no strong Hindutva feelings — Sarna
practices remain central to her spiritual life. Will vote on land/forest right,
not identity.

---

**Persona C: The former CPM panchayat member, now TMC (male, 48-55)**

*Profile:* Bengali-speaking, general caste (Mahishya), has been a panchayat member
since 2008 under CPM. Switched to TMC in 2011 when the Left collapsed. Now manages
a local MGNREGA project and sits on the village forest protection committee.

*Political memory:* Remembers the Maoist period with anxiety — his party office was
burned in 2010, a colleague was killed. Chose to survive by switching allegiance.
Now organically part of the TMC system.

*2019 salience:* Knows the "cut money" system exists — he participates in it (a small
share of every scheme transaction flows through booth committee). Fears BJP because
a BJP win means he loses his position and potentially faces violence. Deeply motivated
to deliver TMC votes.

*Psychological orientation:* Purely transactional. Loyalty to TMC is career survival.
Will work hard to turn out TMC votes from his village, will report troublemakers to
block office. Understands the BJP threat is real in 2019.

---

**Persona D: The BJP Vanvasi Kalyan Ashram affiliate (male, 25-32)**

*Profile:* Santhali youth who stayed at the VKA hostel in Jhargram town during high
school. Has developed a hybrid identity — tribal pride mixed with Hindutva social
network. Educated (class 12), works as a small trader in the weekly market. Active
in BJP booth committee.

*Political memory:* Grew up hearing about Lalgarh but is more influenced by VKA's
narrative: tribal communities are "Vanvasi Hindus" whose forest rights were taken
by the British and continued to be denied by CPM and then TMC. Ram Navami
processions give him a sense of community power.

*2019 salience:* Excited about BJP's 2019 wave. Motivated by "cut money" campaign
— he has personally witnessed the deductions. Distributes WhatsApp forwards about
BJP tribal welfare promises. Also mobilised by the idea of Kumar Hembram — an
IIT-engineer tribal man — as the BJP candidate.

*Psychological orientation:* Enthusiastic BJP voter. Identity aligned with BJP's
Vanvasi Hindu frame. Would be uncomfortable with a formal Sarna-code demand because
it challenges the Hindu incorporation project.

---

**Persona E: The Sarna religion activist (female, 35-45)**

*Profile:* Santhali, Ol Chiki literate, works with an NGO promoting tribal rights
and Sarna dharma recognition. Based in Jhargram town, travels to forest villages
for awareness work. Has participated in All India Sarna Dharma conferences.

*Political memory:* Sees both CPM and TMC as parties that tried to absorb tribal
identity into class politics (CPM) or welfare clientelism (TMC) without recognising
genuine cultural and religious distinctness. Suspicious of BJP's Hinduisation project.

*2019 salience:* Sarna census code is the primary demand. BJP's refusal to formally
commit (despite verbal hints) is deeply troubling. TMC's recognition of Ol Chiki
but rejection of Sarna as a census category is also inadequate. Torn: TMC bad, BJP
worse on identity.

*Psychological orientation:* The most likely abstainer or third-party voter among
major archetypes. If she votes, probably TMC as the lesser identity threat —
despite reservations. Key influencer among Santhali educated women in her network.

---

**Persona F: The school dropout tendu leaf collector (male, 18-22)**

*Profile:* Santhali, dropped out after class 8. Lives in a forest village, collects
tendu leaves every May-June, sal leaves through the year. Aspires to earn Rs 15,000/
month "somehow." Consumes a lot of WhatsApp content.

*Political memory:* No direct memory of Lalgarh (was a child). Politically
unformed. Knows CPM only as "that old party that killed people." TMC is the
current government.

*2019 salience:* Tendu leaf prices paid by the contractor are "too low" and he
knows the contractor is TMC-connected. Would like a regular job — any government
job. Hears BJP promise jobs and schemes on WhatsApp. Cannot evaluate claims
critically.

*Psychological orientation:* Susceptible to BJP's anti-cut-money and "change" 
message. Has no deep ideological commitment. Will vote for whoever appears most
likely to deliver economic change. In 2019, likely BJP.

---

**Persona G: The government primary school teacher (female, 30-40)**

*Profile:* Santhali or general Bengali caste (mixed), government primary teacher
in a forest-block school. Permanent appointment (pre-2011 CPM era or early TMC).
Some connection to TMC via school inspectors and block education officers.

*Political memory:* Appreciates stability. Kanyashree has increased enrolment
dramatically — her classroom is fuller. But SSC (School Service Commission) merit
list irregularities (a growing controversy in 2018-19) make her anxious about
colleagues hired through connections.

*2019 salience:* School runs reasonably well. Concerned about teacher transfers.
Has no cut money complaints of her own but hears about panchayat corruption.
Would prefer continuity over disruption.

*Psychological orientation:* Moderate TMC-leaning but not activist. Security-
oriented voter who fears BJP disruption. Will quietly vote TMC.

---

**Persona H: The tribal contractor, TMC-aligned (male, 40-50)**

*Profile:* Santhali or Munda, but upwardly mobile, running a construction sub-
contract and managing a forest produce depot. Has close ties to TMC block
president. Benefits from the MGNREGA/housing scheme contract ecosystem.

*Political memory:* Made his current position under TMC — no nostalgia for CPM.
The Lalgarh period was dangerous (Maoists levied tributes on contractors). TMC
brought stability and business.

*2019 salience:* A BJP win would dismantle his network. Deeply invested in TMC
result. Organises transport for TMC voters on election day. Manages "cut money"
flow — from his perspective, it is a normal commission for services rendered.

*Psychological orientation:* Hardline TMC. Will do whatever it takes. Represents
the patronage economy that drives anti-incumbency among ordinary tribal voters.

## §9 Vote-rationale shorthand

The following heuristics summarise how each major group made (or will make) their
2019 Lok Sabha vote decision:

**Santhali forest households (core NTFP-dependent):**
> "Didi gave rice and Kanyashree, but the panchayat people stole our MGNREGA money.
> Modi's people say they'll give patta and not take cut money. Maybe try BJP once."
> → Lean BJP, especially cut-money-affected households

**Munda/Ho community with FRA claims outstanding:**
> "The patta has been promised since 2006. Three governments. Still waiting.
> The Forest Department listens to the contractor, not us."
> → High abstention or BJP protest vote

**Kurmi-Mahato households:**
> "We are not OBC, we should be ST. Neither party has delivered this. At least
> BJP has our people in Parliament and Modi talks about tribals."
> → Mixed but leaning BJP given identity politics and anti-TMC sentiment

**SC (Bagdi/Bauri) households in municipality/peri-urban:**
> "Kanyashree, free rice, housing scheme — these are real. TMC is the party of
> the poor in Bengal."
> → TMC-leaning, with some defection where cut money grievances are acute

**Sarna dharma activists and Ol Chiki educated class:**
> "TMC recognises our script but calls us Hindu. BJP calls us Vanvasi Hindu.
> Neither truly respects Sarna. But BJP will hurt us more."
> → Likely TMC or abstention, not BJP

**Bengali non-tribal town residents (Jhargram municipality):**
> "We need development, not politics. Jhargram was always backward. The new
> district is a start. Let's see who can actually develop the region."
> → Mixed; some BJP wave effect in 2019; government employees stay TMC

**Former CPM voters who switched TMC in 2011:**
> "CPM is finished, TMC is what we have. We will not go back. BJP is also risky."
> → Mostly TMC by default, but less enthused than 2011

**Youth (18-25, tribal, low education):**
> "There are no jobs. MGNREGA is all there is. Cut money is real. BJP says change."
> → BJP-leaning, especially males with WhatsApp exposure

**VKA-affiliated tribal youth:**
> "We are Hindu Vanvasi. Ram Navami, Hanuman temple. Modi is building India.
> Mamata is pro-Bangladeshi infiltrator."
> → Strong BJP

**TMC panchayat ecosystem beneficiaries:**
> "Our livelihood depends on TMC winning. Whatever it takes."
> → Mobilised TMC, including booth-rigging pressure

## Appendix: Out of scope

The following events, outcomes, and policy changes occurred **after** the 2019
Lok Sabha election and must NOT be incorporated into persona generation for the
pre-2019 context:

- **COVID-19 pandemic (2020 onward)** — economic shock, migrant return, lockdown
- **Chhatradhar Mahato joining TMC (February 2020)** — he was still in jail
  (released August 2019) and had not yet publicly declared TMC affiliation by
  year-end 2019
- **Sukumar Hansda's death (October 2020)** — he was alive and serving as MLA/
  Deputy Speaker through end-2019
- **Birbaha Hansda joining TMC and contesting 2021 AE** — in 2019, she was a
  Jharkhand Party figure and the TMC LS candidate who lost; her TMC membership
  happened after 2019
- **2021 West Bengal Legislative Assembly election result** (Jhargram won by
  Birbaha Hansda for TMC with 54%)
- **Duare Sarkar scheme (2020-21)** — not yet launched in 2019
- **Sarna census code outcome (2021 census deferred, code still not granted)**
- **Post-2019 BJP consolidation** in Jangalmahal panchayats and the subsequent
  violence in 2021
- **New Forest Rights Act rules or amendments after 2019**
- **Eklavya Model Residential School expansion under 2018 budget** — announced
  but not yet operational in Jhargram by end-2019
- **NRC/CAA protests (December 2019 and after)** — the CAA was passed on
  December 11, 2019; protests in Jhargram (limited, given near-zero Muslim
  population) are on the boundary; do not incorporate post-passage community
  response in Jangalmahal
- **2024 Lok Sabha result** (TMC's Kalipada Soren wins Jhargram LS, reversing 2019)
