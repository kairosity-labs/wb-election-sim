# Cooch Behar Uttar (AC 003) — pre-2019 / end-2019 opinion context

**Purpose.** Narrative anchors for the LLM persona generator (Phase 1.2). Each
persona's psychology, salience topics, and 2019 vote rationale should draw on
the conditions described here. This is **the world the persona lived in
through end-2019** — nothing later.

## 1. Who lives here

**AC 003 Cooch Behar Uttar** sits in the Cooch Behar Sadar sub-division of
Cooch Behar district, in the North Bengal plains of the Terai zone. The
constituency covers the entire Cooch Behar II Community Development Block
(CDB-II) — 13 gram panchayats and 5 census towns (Khagrabari, Takagachh,
Chakchaka, Baisguri, Baneswar). Total electorate ~2.72 lakh in 2019; turnout
85% in the 2019 LS.

The dominant community is the **Koch-Rajbanshi** (Rajbanshi), who constitute
~38–40% of the total population and ~85% of the Scheduled Caste pool. The
AC is SC-reserved. Muslims form ~17% of the population — predominantly
Bengali-Sheikh peasantry, locally settled for generations, concentrated in
the rural gram panchayats. A thin Hindi/Marwari trader fringe (~2%) occupies
the census-town commercial belts around Khagrabari and Takagachh. The
constituency is overwhelmingly Bengali-speaking (~93%), with a distinct
Rajbongshi / Kamtapuri dialect sub-layer (~1–2% mother-tongue speakers,
with ~7–10% bilingual functionality among Rajbanshi SC households).

Cooch Behar was a **princely state** until 1949 — the Koch Rajbanshi royal
house ruled for centuries before the state acceded to India under the
Maharaja Jagaddipendra Narayan by agreement on 28 August 1949. The merger
is a live political grievance: GCPA factions argue the agreement guaranteed
cultural autonomy and economic development that was never delivered. This
historical consciousness sets Cooch Behar apart from the rest of Bengal
even at the voter psychology level — Rajbanshi residents commonly describe
themselves as "the original people of this land."

Cooch Behar Uttar's rural face is paddy-farming and agricultural labour,
with tobacco cultivation as the distinctive cash crop. The census-town
cluster has a small commercial and service economy anchored by highway
trade. Unemployment among educated youth (graduates, Higher Secondary
pass) is a persistent structural grievance.

## 2. The 2019 LS political shock

The 2019 Lok Sabha result for Cooch Behar (PC 1, SC-reserved) was among the
most dramatic in North Bengal:

  - **Nisith Pramanik (BJP)**: 732,594 votes — **47.98%**, won
  - **Paresh Chandra Adhikary (AITC)**: 677,363 — **44.43%**, lost
  - **Margin**: 54,231 votes / 3.55 pp (BJP over AITC)

Within AC 003 Cooch Behar Uttar specifically, BJP's margin was even larger:
**51.27% BJP vs 39.50% AITC** (BJP margin 11.77 pp, 27,226 votes). The
Forward Bloc — which had held this AC as a Left bastion through 2016 — was
reduced to 4.28% (AIFB). The INC received 2.45%. This is a structural
collapse of a 30-year political order.

**Why the 2019 swing was so dramatic:**

1. Cooch Behar was a Forward Bloc fortress from 1977 to 2009 at the LS level
   (32 years unbroken). TMC dislodged Left in 2014 as part of the national
   Left collapse; BJP had only ~16% in 2014. In AC 003's Assembly elections,
   Forward Bloc's Nagendranath Roy had actually *strengthened* his hold,
   winning in 2011 (45.11%, a narrow margin) and again in 2016 (43.63%,
   12,293 vote margin) even as AITC swept Bengal. The Rajbanshi vote in
   this AC was the last holdout of Left-Rajbanshi alignment.

2. **Nisith Pramanik's entry** changed the calculus entirely. Pramanik, a
   former TMC youth wing leader expelled in 2018 for running independent
   candidates against official TMC nominees in panchayat polls, crossed over
   to BJP. He is himself from the Rajbanshi community — the first Rajbanshi to
   subsequently become a Union Cabinet Minister. His identity gave BJP a face
   that could speak directly to the Rajbanshi SC base in their idiom.

3. **GCPA alliance with BJP.** Ananta Rai Maharaj's faction of the Greater
   Cooch Behar People's Association (which had allied informally with BJP
   in 2016) converted this into a coherent Rajbanshi-identity coalition by
   2019. With GCPA yellow flags appearing at BJP rallies and Ananta Rai's
   backing, Rajbanshi voters who had never identified with BJP's Hindutva
   superstructure could vote BJP as a vote for their own community's leader
   and statehood aspiration.

4. The **2018 panchayat violence** — in which BJP polling agents were shot
   and booths were captured in Cooch Behar, and Pramanik himself was expelled
   for organizing resistance — sharpened anti-TMC anger among Rajbanshi voters
   who felt the TMC treated North Bengal as an extraction zone, not a political
   constituency.

State-wide context: BJP went from 2 → 18 LS seats in Bengal; TMC held 22
(down from 34). North Bengal had 8 of 18 BJP victories. Cooch Behar, once
a Left+TMC region, became a BJP stronghold in one election cycle.

## 3. The Rajbanshi identity axis — the load-bearing narrative of this AC

No other constituency in this simulation has a single community-identity
story as structurally central as the Koch-Rajbanshi axis in Cooch Behar.
Understanding it is essential for generating realistic personas.

**Who are the Rajbanshi?** The Koch-Rajbanshi are an indigenous North Bengal
community who trace their origins to the Koch tribal kingdom that founded
the Kamata-Cooch Behar state in the 16th century. They are classified as
Scheduled Caste in West Bengal (uniquely — they are OBC in Assam and ST in
Meghalaya). The Cooch Behar royal family was Koch Rajbanshi; the Rajbanshi
formed ~89% of the princely state's population at merger.

**The Kshatriya movement.** In the late 19th century, the great Rajbanshi
social reformer **Thakur Panchanan Barma** (1866–1935) led a "Rajbanshi
Kshatriya movement" — asserting that Rajbanshis were not low-caste but
descended from the Kshatriya varna, wrongly demoted by Brahmanical
classification. This assertion of royal/warrior lineage is deeply embedded
in Rajbanshi collective memory: calling a Rajbanshi "low caste" is a
provocation. The university in Cooch Behar is named after Panchanan Barma.
His image appears in homes alongside deities. His birthday (14 February) is
a community observance. Any Rajbanshi persona will carry a consciousness of
this dignity claim even if they can't articulate it textually.

**The Greater Cooch Behar / Kamatapur movements.** Two overlapping statehood
movements draw on the same Rajbanshi identity pool:
  - **Greater Cooch Behar People's Association (GCPA)**, led by Ananta Rai
    Maharaj, demands Union Territory or separate state status for Cooch Behar,
    citing the 1949 merger agreement. Ananta Rai aligned with BJP by 2016–19.
  - **Kamatapur movement** (broader — seeks a state comprising Cooch Behar,
    Jalpaiguri, Alipurduar, Darjeeling and parts of Assam's Koch-Rajbanshi
    belt). Its Bangshi Badan Barman faction has leaned TMC.

In 2019, the GCPA-BJP alignment meant that a vote for Nisith Pramanik
felt, to many Rajbanshi voters, like a vote for their own statehood
aspiration, their community's dignity, and a rejection of the TMC's
indifference to their ethnic demands. This fusion — Rajbanshi identity +
BJP + statehood demand — is the master key to understanding the 2019 swing.

**Important nuance for persona generation:** the Rajbanshi community is
*not monolithic*. The Kamatapur movement's Barman faction is anti-BJP. Young
educated Rajbanshis in census towns sometimes find the "Greater Cooch Behar"
demand parochial. Rajbanshi women — who may hold Kanyashree and Swasthya
Sathi cards — are more split than Rajbanshi men. Don't generate every
Rajbanshi persona as a uniform BJP voter; the 65% Rajbanshi BJP share
implies 35% voted AITC, AIFB, or others.

## 4. The NRC / CAA axis — a more complex story here than in Matua belt

The NRC/CAA narrative in Cooch Behar is *structurally different* from South
Bengal's Matua belt (AC 095 Bangaon Uttar). Both communities are Hindu SC;
both had reasons to care about citizenship legislation. But the local meaning
is almost opposite in important dimensions.

**The Assam NRC shock (Aug 2019).** The final Assam NRC published 31 August
2019 excluded ~1.9 million people — of whom a substantial fraction were
Bengali Hindus (estimates put the Bengali Hindu exclusion at ~500,000, with
Rajbanshis specifically exposed). For Rajbanshi families in Cooch Behar,
many of whom have kin in Assam, this was terrifying. But it was also
paradoxically *confirming of the BJP promise*: the logic was "we need CAA
to protect Bengali Hindus from NRC exercises." By end-2019, the BJP narrative
was: "NRC is coming; CAA gives Hindu refugees protection first."

**The Rajbanshi's ambivalent position on CAA.** Unlike Matuas (who are
unambiguously refugees who need citizenship), the Rajbanshi split into two
camps:
  - **Indigenous Rajbanshis** (the majority, locally settled for generations,
    no Bangladesh-origin ancestry) see CAA as *potentially threatening*:
    it regularizes large numbers of Bengali Hindu refugees as new citizens,
    diluting the indigenous Rajbanshi demographic share. Their ancestors
    watched Cooch Behar's Rajbanshi population decline from ~47% in 1951 to
    ~39% by 1991 as refugees arrived. More new settlers = more erosion.
  - **Refugee-origin Rajbanshis** (a smaller fraction, or Rajbanshis with
    Bangladeshi family connections) see CAA as protection.

This internal tension means Rajbanshis who voted BJP in 2019 were *not*
primarily voting for CAA — they were voting for Rajbanshi community
consolidation, the GCPA statehood alliance, and anti-TMC sentiment. The
BJP's Rajbanshi vote and the Matua CAA vote come from different psychological
logics even though they look similar in vote-share tables.

**TMC's response.** Mamata Banerjee's line — "no NRC in Bengal under any
circumstances" — resonated somewhat with indigenous Rajbanshis who feared
being asked to prove identity. The TMC also emphasized: "Rajbanshis are
already citizens of India. They have no need for CAA." This gave Rajbanshi
voters a permission structure to stay with TMC (or return to it) if they
prioritised the indigeneity/CAA-ambivalence axis.

**End-2019 persona state of mind on citizenship:** A Rajbanshi voter in
December 2019 — after the CAA has passed but rules are not yet notified —
might hold these simultaneously:
  - "Modi won; the BJP has passed the CAA as promised."
  - "But I don't know if CAA helps me or hurts me by bringing more outsiders."
  - "At least Nisith is our own man, a Rajbanshi, in Delhi."
  - "NRC is scary — what if they come here next?"

This psychological ambivalence distinguishes the Cooch Behar Rajbanshi
voter from both the Matua (who wanted CAA for paperless citizenship) and
the Muslim (who was uniformly opposed to CAA).

## 5. The 2015 enclave exchange and its local residue

In 2015, the India–Bangladesh Land Boundary Agreement resolved the 68-year
tangle of 162 "chitmahals" (enclaves) — small patches of Bangladesh inside
India and vice versa. Of these, virtually all Indian-side enclaves lay within
Cooch Behar district. The exchange added ~14,000 former Bangladeshi-enclave
residents as new Indian citizens in Cooch Behar; approximately 900 families
came to India from what became Bangladeshi territories and were placed in
rehabilitation camps.

For AC 003 voters, this is recent lived experience, not ancient history.
By 2019 these "new citizens" are present in the area — a visible marker of
how the border can change one's citizenship status overnight. For indigenous
Rajbanshi voters who already feel demographically squeezed, the enclave
exchange added a low-level anxiety about who counts as "original." BJP
politicians selectively invoked the Teen Bigha corridor as a "territorial
concession" to Bangladesh, feeding a border-insecurity narrative.

For Muslim voters with Bangladesh-area family connections, the enclave
exchange was broadly neutral to positive — it resolved ambiguous legal
status for border-area families.

## 6. The Changrabandha border economy

**AC 003 is not the Changrabandha land port AC** — the ICP is in Cooch
Behar Sadar / Mekhliganj territory, roughly 30–35 km north of Khagrabari.
But its shadow falls on the entire district economy.

Changrabandha is a crucial gateway for India–Bangladesh–Bhutan trilateral
trade. Daily truck crossings number 350–375 (India-Bangladesh corridor);
~90 trucks/day also pass through to Bhutan. Major exports include stone
boulders, coal, jute seeds, wheat, tobacco (steam-cured), plywood and resin.
Major imports include cotton waste, RMG, food products, and building
materials. An ADB 2021 estimate placed Changrabandha-Burimari as handling
7.1% of Bangladesh's total land-based exports to India — a significant
fraction of a bilateral trade relationship measured in billions of dollars.

The local economy of AC 003 connects to this trade via:
  - **Transport and logistics workers** in Khagrabari and Takagachh census
    towns — drivers, loaders, clearing agents who work the highway corridor.
  - **Tobacco trade** — Cooch Behar is West Bengal's largest tobacco producer;
    tobacco grown in the AC's rural panchayats moves through Changrabandha
    to Bangladesh.
  - **Retail / wholesale trade** — Khagrabari's commercial cluster serves
    as a distribution node for goods moving off the Changrabandha-NH-31C
    route.
  - **Informal cross-border economic ties** — particularly for Muslim
    families with Bangladesh-origin relatives; small-scale informal trade,
    remittances, and family visits are a background economic reality.

The Changrabandha ICP modernisation (Integrated Check Post under LPAI)
was underway in 2019 — a BJP-era infrastructure project that local traders
mentioned positively. But infrastructure delays, bureaucratic clearance
backlogs, and lack of cold-storage facilities were persistent complaints.

## 7. The tobacco and agriculture economy

Cooch Behar district is **West Bengal's largest tobacco-producing district** —
a fact that appears on competitive exam syllabi and is a source of
local pride. The Dinhata Tobacco Research Station (established 1951) has
been a hub for cigar-tobacco varieties suited to the Cooch Behar sub-tropical
climate. The fertile, well-drained loamy soil across the CDB-II block is
well-suited to both tobacco and jute.

For AC 003's farming households, this creates an economic profile:
  - **Rice** (kharif + boro) is the staple; most small-cultivator households
    grow paddy as subsistence plus sale.
  - **Tobacco** is the cash crop — higher-value than jute or paddy, but
    requires more labour, working capital, and market access. Tobacco steam
    export through Changrabandha to Bangladesh is a known market.
  - **Jute** has been in structural decline due to fluctuating prices but
    remains part of the rotation for some households.
  - **Agricultural labour** (~32% of workers) does daily-wage work on
    others' farms for paddy harvest, tobacco leaf-picking, and jute
    processing; wages ~Rs 200–300/day in 2019.

**Krishak Bandhu** (launched January 2019 by TMC) provided Rs 5,000/acre/year
to cultivator households + Rs 2 lakh death cover — arriving just months
before the LS election. In the district's Krishak Bandhu portal, Cooch Behar
shows high enrollment. But many tobacco farmers' principal grievance is about
*prices and market access* — the lack of a functioning FMC auction platform
for tobacco in Bengal means farmers are dependent on middlemen (aratdars)
who set prices. This buyer-power squeeze is a background economic frustration
that political parties exploited without resolving.

The fringe of Dooars tea gardens begins east of Cooch Behar, primarily in
adjacent Alipurduar and Jalpaiguri districts. AC 003 itself has minimal
direct tea-garden employment; but the tea-belt political culture (Left Front
historically strong among tribal Dooars tea workers) provides regional
context. Tea-belt workers' wages (~Rs 250/day in 2019) are a point of
comparison that tobacco and paddy farmers make when calibrating their
own economic grievances.

## 8. Political violence, anti-TMC anger, and the 2018 panchayat memory

The 2018 panchayat elections left deep scars in Cooch Behar. Reports from
May 2018 documented:
  - Two people dead, 20+ injured in Cooch Behar district clashes.
  - BJP polling agents shot at booths in Cooch Behar, with police standing
    by.
  - Booth capturing, ballot-box destruction, and rigging in Dinhata and
    Mathabhanga blocks (neighbouring ACs).
  - TMC winning 34% of seats across WB *uncontested*, allegedly because
    opposition candidates were coerced into not filing nominations.

Nisith Pramanik was directly involved in the resistance — he organized
~300 independent candidates to run against TMC nominees, was expelled
from TMC for it, and crossed over to BJP. For Rajbanshi voters in AC 003,
the 2018 panchayat violence was not abstract: many had participated in the
panchayat process, seen intimidation, or knew workers who were attacked.

By 2019, "vote for us or face the consequences" had been the TMC message in
North Bengal for two election cycles. The BJP offered a vehicle for anger
that the Forward Bloc — credibility damaged by national Left collapse —
could no longer provide. The scale of BJP's 2019 win in AC 003 (11.77 pp
over AITC, with AIFB collapsing to 4.28%) reflects this consolidated
anti-TMC vote.

## 9. Media diet (light brushstrokes)

  - **Bengali print**: Anandabazar Patrika (dominant, though less penetration
    in rural North Bengal than South Bengal), Uttarbanga Sambad (North Bengal
    specific — key local paper for Cooch Behar), Dainik Sambad, Aajkaal.
    Uttarbanga Sambad is the first paper locals reach for district politics.
  - **TV**: ABP Ananda (most-watched Bengali news), Zee 24 Ghanta, News18
    Bangla. North Bengal has slightly lower TV saturation (~74% of HH) than
    South Bengal urban areas, but television is the dominant news medium for
    older voters and women.
  - **WhatsApp**: Growing rapidly with Jio-era smartphone penetration (~40%
    smartphone-internet in AC 003 by 2019). BJP had active WhatsApp
    networks in Cooch Behar coordinated through Pramanik's circle.
    GCPA-affiliated groups also used WhatsApp for Rajbanshi community
    mobilisation (event notices for Koch-Rajbanshi cultural observances,
    Panchanan Barma jayanti, Greater Cooch Behar rallies).
  - **National TV** (cable households): Republic TV (pre-Republic Bangla, so
    Hindi-medium, less reach than in 2021+), NDTV, ABP News. National Modi
    narrative largely mediated through Bengali channels.
  - **Radio**: Minimal, declining.
  - **Local influencer network**: Rajbanshi community leaders, GCPA branch
    workers, and panchayat-level BJP booth committees operate as word-of-mouth
    media in rural GPs where smartphone penetration is lower (~22–30% for
    agricultural-labourer households).

## 10. Common psychological archetypes (6–8 personas)

Use these as starting templates — vary, blend, and complicate with the
demographic axes the persona holds:

  - **The Rajbanshi elder patriarch (60+, male, rural GP, cultivator):**
    Voted Left for decades — remembers Nagendranath Roy as "our man" in the
    Assembly, the Forward Bloc as the party that "at least came from the
    poor." Crossed to BJP in 2019 after the 2018 panchayat violence convinced
    him TMC was Kolkata's party, not his. Carries the Panchanan Barma dignity
    claim in his bones: "We built this kingdom; we are not migrants." Has
    heard of NRC; is nervous; trusts Pramanik as "one of ours" to protect.
    Does not understand CAA paperwork but knows the slogan.

  - **The Rajbanshi young man (25–35, male, rural GP, educated-unemployed or
    tobacco-labourer):** Pass or HS degree from the local school, no
    government job yet; does tobacco or paddy labour seasonally. Angry
    at both TMC ("they only care about their cut from panchayat contracts")
    and the system generally. Responded to Nisith Pramanik's energy and
    youth — first time he felt someone like him was running for the national
    parliament. Voted BJP in 2019 as much out of excitement at Pramanik's
    candidacy as ideology. Ambivalent about CAA: knows Rajbanshis are "already
    citizens" but the slogan matters as symbolic recognition.

  - **The Rajbanshi woman beneficiary (35–50, female, rural GP, agricultural
    family):** Kanyashree cardholder for her daughter; household has
    Swasthya Sathi card. Krishak Bandhu deposit arrived in her husband's
    account in 2019. Has a direct, personal relationship with TMC schemes
    even as her husband voted BJP. Doesn't follow statehood politics; her
    voting frame is "who gave me something concrete." Split-household scenario
    common.

  - **The Bengali-Sheikh Muslim farmer (40s, male, rural GP border area):**
    Family settled in the same CDB-II gram panchayat for three generations.
    Documents are in order, but the CAA passage in December 2019 makes him
    uneasy — not about his own status but about what comes next. "First they
    said CAA is only for refugees. Then what's NRC for?" Votes TMC with
    strong conviction; has no interest in either AIFB or INC after their
    collapse. Quietly proud that Mamata blocked NRC announcement.

  - **The Khagrabari census-town trader (40s, male, Hindi/Bengali bilingual
    or Rajbanshi small businessman):** Runs a wholesale fertiliser or FMCG
    shop. Uses the Changrabandha highway trade indirectly — buys from
    Bangladeshi-import distributors. Demonetisation (2016) was painful; GST
    (2017) added compliance overhead. By 2019 business is stable. Modi-BJP
    aligned nationally; ambivalent at state level but does not want TMC's
    panchayat-level "cuts" on business. Voted BJP in 2019.

  - **The AIFB legacy voter (55+, male, rural GP, marginal-cultivator
    Rajbanshi or OBC):** Voted Forward Bloc since 1977; knows Nagendranath Roy
    personally (helped at his booth). Could not bring himself to vote AIFB
    in 2019 after AIFB was reduced to a rump nationally but also could not
    vote BJP ("too Hindu, too Kolkata"). Voted AITC reluctantly as a
    "lesser evil." Or stayed home. The AIFB collapse from 44% (2016 AE) to
    4.28% (2019 LS) in this AC concentrates largely in this persona — some
    voted AITC, some BJP, some NOTA or IND.

  - **The Pundibari / Madhupur GP Rajbanshi woman (30s, female, rural, SC):**
    Has some familiarity with GCPA meetings — her male relatives attended
    Greater Cooch Behar cultural events. Voted BJP in 2019 but primarily
    because "everyone in the village said to vote for Nisith-da." Less
    politically opinionated than the men; knows welfare schemes by name;
    thinks "didi has done some things but has not done things for us
    Rajbanshis." Open to returning to AITC if an acceptable Rajbanshi
    candidate emerged.

  - **The educated-unemployed graduate in Khagrabari census town (22–28,
    male, Rajbanshi SC):** B.A. graduate, three years of WB group-D/WBPSC
    prep, works part-time at a mobile recharge shop. Angry at TMC's
    panchayat-level nepotism in job allocation ("you need a party card to
    get anything"). Voted BJP in 2019 as protest-identity vote. CAA is
    secondary; what matters to him is a job in the system he can reach on
    merit. Follows national news on smartphone; is the main information
    conduit from WhatsApp to his parents.

## 11. Vote-rationale shorthand for end-2019

Map common 2019-LS rationales the LLM can draw on. The persona's demographic
profile + this rationale should feel mutually consistent.

  - **BJP (51.27% in AC 003)** — Rajbanshi community consolidation behind
