## [2023-12-31] (reflection, importance 8)
I thought the CAA would finally make us proper citizens, that someone in power would remember the Matuas who crossed with nothing and built this land with our own hands. But what I learned that year is that all these big men—Shantanu, Abhishek, whoever—they fight over temples and power while my grandson still goes to bed with hunger in his belly, and my roof still leaks when the monsoon comes. The lotus button I pressed so carefully, thinking it was for people like us—it turned out to be just another promise that forgets the small people once the voting ends.

## [2024-01-31] (reaction, importance 5)

They all wear different colors and make different promises, but none of them remember that we are still here, still waiting.

## [2024-01-31] (reaction, importance 5)

But tell me, does a temple in Ayodhya fix the leak in my roof? Does it put rice in my grandson's belly? I pressed the lotus button thinking they remembered us Matuas, the ones who came with nothing and built this land. Now I see—the big men remember the gods, but they forget the small people who still wait for a proper paper that says we belong here.

## [2024-02-29] (reaction, importance 6)

## [2024-02-29] (reaction, importance 6)

Just like I am waiting for my papers, for my roof to be fixed. These fights between TMC and BJP, they are not my fight. Both have forgotten me.

## [2024-03-31] (reaction, importance 3)

## [2024-04-30] (reaction, importance 4)
So the teachers—twenty-five thousand of them—they paid money, they got jobs, and now the court says no, those jobs are gone like water through a sieve. The big men in courts and governments, they take and they take, and the small people lose what little they had. This is what I have seen my whole life—whether it is the lotus people or the green flag people, the system eats the poor. My roof still leaks. My grandson still goes hungry. One more broken promise to young people does not surprise me anymore.

## [2024-05-20] (reaction, importance 6)
Fourteen papers? Fourteen? My son read it to me—fourteen physical certificates, a few hundred digital ones they say. Five years they promised, and now five days before the election they give fourteen papers to show the people. I ask my son: am I one of the fourteen? He does not know. The Matua people are celebrating on the television, but I have learned not to celebrate until the paper is in my hands, until I can touch it. Fourteen papers for how many Matuas who crossed with nothing? This is not a promise kept—this is a show for the voting booth.
